Interview Notes

Thematic analysis drawn from recorded interviews with six participants — exploring the problems, needs, and platform directions that shaped the AIUK Community Platform project.

Introduction

This document presents insights gathered through a series of recorded interviews with participants involved in the Community Platform project. We hosted six interviews and created audio recordings that were then transcribed using Sonix.ai, providing an accurate written record of each conversation. See our User Research Guide for more information about the methodology of the research and other components of it.

To identify key themes, we ran transcripts through a large language model (LLM) and prompted it to help us pull out various themes. We took care to maintain privacy throughout this process; no personal identifiers were included in the thematic analysis, and all data handling was managed with discretion. Human reviewers oversaw each stage, ensuring that the findings were both accurate and respectful of participants' confidentiality.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Original on Google Drive (unavailable after May 2026)

Grouping Problems, Needs, and Directions

The interview findings are organised into three broad categories: the problems activists currently face, the needs a new platform must address, and the directions that point toward effective solutions. Each category draws on consistent patterns across all six interviews.

⚠️

Problems

Structural and practical barriers that fragment the activist community and limit effective organising.

🎯

Needs

What activists and staff require from a community platform to participate fully and effectively.

💡

Directions

Solution and feature directions that emerged from across the interviews as potential ways forward.

Problems

Fragmentation and Isolation

  • Communication and resources are scattered across multiple platforms and tools (for example email, WhatsApp, Teams, Facebook and even print). This results in confusion, missed updates, and duplication.
  • Siloed groups (by geography, theme, or function) make it hard to collaborate, cross-pollinate, and knowledge-share.
  • Local groups often feel isolated, unable to easily connect or discover what others are doing.

Generational and Digital Literacy Gaps

  • Older or less tech-confident members struggle with modern platforms; digital exclusion is not only about age but also about differing comfort levels and access.
  • Reliance on email or print for some, versus real-time apps for others, highlights a cultural and generational divide.
  • Attempts to move to new platforms can alienate legacy members and risk losing institutional knowledge.

Inaccessible Design and Buried Accessibility Features

  • Many platforms hide accessibility tools, making them hard to use for disabled and neurodiverse activists.
  • Accessibility standards are inconsistently met, with poor support for screen-readers, alternate fonts, and image descriptions.
  • Print, email, and exportable content are still essential for full participation, yet often overlooked in digital-first solutions.

Centralised Staff-Control and Bureaucratic Bottlenecks

  • Amnesty's heavy reliance on staff for facilitation and decision-making delays or inhibits community-driven initiatives.
  • Formerly effective tools for mapping, connecting, or responding locally have vanished, replaced by processes that must go through the office.
  • Groups cannot easily contact others in their area or by shared interest, and often must wait on central office approval.

Ineffective Moderation, Safety, and Privacy Tools

  • Online abuse, doxxing, and trolling are frequently mentioned, particularly on contentious topics. Moderation is often absent, slow, or poorly designed.
  • Public-facing activism can put personal safety at risk, and pseudonyms or anonymity are not always adequately supported.
  • Volunteer moderation does not have enough capacity, which leads to unresolved conflicts or slow intervention when issues arise.

Outdated or Over-Complex Digital Infrastructure

  • Amnesty's website and various platforms lack intuitive navigation, self-publishing, or simple group creation options.
  • Too many features, tabs, or video-heavy designs alienate users both young and old.
  • Notification systems are either overwhelming (spam) or ineffective, resulting in poor engagement.

Needs

Centralisation with Flexibility

  • A unified community platform to bring together events, resources, conversations, and group directories, while allowing for easy segmentation by interest, region, and privacy level.
  • Searchable, browsable "home" for activism, encouraging peer learning and resource sharing.
  • Ability to create and self-organise subgroups without staff gatekeeping.

Multimodal, Accessible Communication

  • Integration for print, email, and digital (web/app) options so all members can participate and keep up to date, regardless of digital skills.
  • Export and print functions, email summaries, and batch notifications to support both digital and offline engagement.
  • Accessibility by default: built-in text-to-speech, alt-text prompts, colour/font adjustments, culturally clear icons, and easy-to-read formats.

Stronger Moderation, Safety, and Privacy Options

  • Context-sensitive moderation which balances open debate with providing a safe space.
  • Tools to allow anonymous posting (visible to the group, accountable on the backend), strong privacy settings, and backend tracking for accountability.
  • Paid or professional moderation support to supplement volunteers in managing and supporting large groups.

Engagement, Recognition, and Progression Pathways

  • Learning pathways, open badges, or recognition systems for activist development and increased engagement over time.
  • Features that support onboarding, mentorship, and celebrating contributions (peer-to-peer as well as staff-recognised).

Facilitated Peer-to-Peer Connection and Mapping

  • Directories, mapping, and "find other activists like me" tools for location, skills, or themes.
  • Resource directories with easy templates for event promotion, campaign launches, and mutual support.
  • Capacity for outreach in different ways (e.g. direct, local, and thematic) without bottlenecks.

Solution / Feature Directions

User-Driven, Intuitive Design

  • Simple, clear interface usable by people with varying digital skills (e.g. avoid bloat and unnecessary tabs).
  • Optional templates for posts/events with built-in accessibility features (e.g. alt text).
  • Ability for activists, not just staff, to create, manage, and moderate groups (and to onboard new members).

Hybrid Public and Private Spaces

  • Model after Discord/Teams approach: some open, browsable areas; others invite-only or approval-based.
  • Allow for activists to subscribe to announcements-only and/or discussion groups to suit different engagement preferences.

Notification and Reminder Controls

  • Fine-tuned, user-controllable notifications ("nudge not spam"); options for digest/emails or real-time alerts.
  • Features for engagement prompts so that activists can engage periodically without becoming overwhelmed.

Onboarding, Facilitation, and the Human Touch

  • Support for mentoring/buddy systems and "welcome" pathways to avoid leaving new community members isolated.
  • Active facilitation to encourage stronger group connections, especially during initial rollout.

Respect for Legacy and Real-World Engagement

  • Maintain and honour print-based and in-person engagement; support blended approaches, not just digital.
  • Celebrate and retain the stories, knowledge, and techniques of longstanding members and leaders.

Summary of Themes

Across all six interviews, ten cross-cutting themes emerged. These themes represent the most consistent and strongly held views from participants.

  1. Staff-Centric Structures and Community Autonomy
    • Several participants flag that Amnesty's support and communication are often overly staff-mediated, which stifles organic growth, slows down decision-making, and creates unnecessary bottlenecks for local groups and thematic networks.
    • There is a strong call for a shift towards empowering activists to organise, connect, and create subgroups independently — without waiting for central office facilitation or approval.
    • Past pilot efforts (e.g., NationBuilder for speaker matching) worked well when they minimised staff gatekeeping, but were discontinued, leaving a significant gap.
  2. Recognition, Progression, and Activist Learning Pathways
    • Some participants articulate a desire for structures supporting activist recognition and development (e.g., badges, learning pathways, progression after training).
    • These elements are seen as a way to sustain engagement and help community members transition from entry-level involvement to leadership or specialist roles, particularly in educator and campaigner networks.
  3. Connecting Across Silos (Thematic, Role, Geographical)
    • There is a notable missed opportunity to connect activists outside their initial "entry point" (e.g., fundraising, teaching, student, local group). This leads to functional and geographic silos, resulting in duplication of effort and isolated communities.
    • Participants want platforms that make it easy to find and connect with others based on shared interests, locality, roles, or campaign themes — not just replicate existing hierarchies.
  4. Notification Management and Engagement Nudges
    • Both staff and activists mention struggles with notification settings: too much "noise" (spam) or nothing at all leads to disengagement.
    • The most successful platforms (including those for parent/teacher communities) provide just-enough nudges to keep people informed without overwhelming them.
    • Participants request better notification and reminder controls, especially for less frequent participants or those who "dip in and out" of activism.
  5. Balancing Visibility, Anonymity, and Accountability
    • There is a clear need for features that support anonymous posting or pseudonyms for those at higher risk, balanced by back-end accountability for staff to manage safety and abuse.
    • Activists report being targeted, doxxed, or harassed — requiring options for safeguarding both their digital and offline identities. At the same time, complete anonymity is not workable for organisational trust and moderation.
  6. Platform Simplicity, Template Use, and Avoiding Over-Complexity
    • Simplicity and clarity trump feature-bloat: ideal systems are described as intuitive, focused, and usable even by those with limited digital experience.
    • Practical tools like post templates (e.g., with mandatory accessibility or event details) are highly valued, reducing cognitive load and making sure posts are actionable and inclusive.
    • Several participants point out that elaborate, "shiny" platforms with lots of tabs or video-heavy designs are exclusionary both in practice and perception.
  7. Community Mapping and Direct Local Outreach
    • The need for mapping tools — connecting people by location, theme, or professional background — comes through repeatedly. Past systems enabled local speaker or activist matching but have not been replaced since being retired.
    • Direct local outreach (for events, rapid mobilisation, or peer support) is widely requested but currently bottlenecked by central coordination.
  8. Facilitation, Active Onboarding, and Human Touch
    • Repeated across interviews is the lesson that community platforms require active facilitation, onboarding, and sometimes even one-to-one introductions to be effective — especially during initial adoption.
    • Successful peer sharing and cross-group learning are more likely when people are "taken under the wing" or nudged to participate, rather than left to discover everything passively.
  9. Balancing Online and Offline Community Life
    • There is subtle recognition that even the best digital platform will not replace the value of in-person meetings, printed materials, or spontaneous local activism.
    • The new platform should support — not override — these real-world patterns, offering bridges (such as print/export functions and synchronised event calendars) rather than forcing everyone into a single digital mode.
  10. Professional and Organisational Networks Beyond Volunteers
    • Some interviews highlight gaps in connecting professional constituencies (e.g., teachers, lawyers, cultural workers) who engage through their organisational roles rather than as individual activists.
    • There is an appetite for platforms that allow these users to connect, share resources, and collaborate independently of staff, while meeting relevant safeguarding and privacy expectations.

Individual Interview Notes

The following section presents notes from each of the six interviews, preserving the detail and texture of individual perspectives. All participant names have been redacted; participants are referred to by number only.

Interview 6

Participant 6

Introduction to Activism and Community Roots

  • Participant 6 first engaged with Amnesty at age 14, prompted by a family member's light involvement and a personal interest, and now represents both Midlands and disabled students on the Student Action Network's national committee.
  • Their role involves bridging local (older, offline) and national (student, online) segments, revealing a broad perspective on inter-group communication needs and digital divides.
  • Their activism is voluntary and committee-based, motivated by a wish to participate directly in the organisation's networks, rather than only signing petitions or donating.

Use of Digital Tools and Communication Patterns

  • Amnesty organising among students is anchored in WhatsApp, supplemented by periodic use of Microsoft Teams for more structured activity; local groups, on the other hand, rely almost exclusively on email and in-person meetings.
  • Their core university interaction takes place via Discord, organised through their course and the students' union, where channel-based, threaded discussions enable complex topic management and community cohesion.
  • They describe a pragmatic approach — choosing platforms based on audience and purpose, such as email for older members and in-person meetings, while using instant messaging and social media for peer networks.

Moderation, Safety, and Peer Management

  • While they have not been personally targeted online, they are aware of heated moments and "server splits" in Discord communities, often resolved by moderators asking participants to "be nice" rather than using technical enforcement.
  • They have not acted as a moderator but do function as a feedback conduit between students and institutional staff, demonstrating a facilitation role in digital environments.
  • Amnesty has provided them with activist safety training and GDPR guidance, though the details of these sessions were not fully retained, with some sessions missed.

Platform Structure, Features, and Discoverability

  • They highlight the organisational benefits of Teams and Discord: a single "server" or "team" can contain multiple channels grouped by theme or cohort, reducing cross-topic confusion and helping members find relevant discussions.
  • The ease of spinning up WhatsApp groups is countered by the lack of discoverability — users can only join by invitation, which fragments communities and hides discussions from those who might benefit.
  • They recommend a hybrid approach: platforms should offer both public and private spaces, allow directory browsing or approval-based entry, and support both open and invite-only channels, drawing on Discord's model.

Content Sharing and Activity Patterns

  • Content resharing is most valuable within the context of an internal platform, increasing awareness of activities across sections rather than amplifying to the general public.
  • They express uncertainty about the relevance of "liking" or "reposting" mechanics in a closed activist platform, suggesting that distribution needs are different from those on mainstream social media.
  • Threaded conversations within channels are identified as essential for keeping discussion focused and avoiding topic confusion, particularly in technical or campaign contexts.

Accessibility and Inclusive Design

  • Strongly advocates for built-in accessibility: text-to-speech, compliance with digital accessibility standards, and easy-to-use image description prompts that are surfaced (not hidden).
  • Highlights the inadequacy of many platforms' accessibility "buttons," proposing explicit workflows that prompt or require users to either describe images or note when descriptions are declined.
  • Suggests customisable visual options (colour schemes, fonts), cultural accessibility (understandable icons and BSL options), and even AI-driven support for one-to-one or small group meetings, referencing best practice from student union sites.

Multimodal Communication and Intergenerational Connection

  • They are acutely aware of the average age of local group members (estimated 60+), and address the need for systems that combine modern, digital-first features with support for low-tech users.
  • Proposes "email portal" style gateways, producing summaries of digital chats for delivery as emails (with reply-to-email functionality), enabling ongoing participation for those who do not use web-based or app-based tools.
  • Recommends print-friendly and exportable message threads (such as a "print last 50 messages" button), which would facilitate in-person discussion and allow members to access information offline, recognising that paper materials remain a community norm.

Resource Discovery and Cross-Network Learning

  • They find out about new campaigns and resources through peer networks in group chats, as well as via casual browsing of social media feeds (e.g. Facebook, Instagram).
  • Visibility is shaped by what appears in these peer spaces, rather than any centralised Amnesty platform, reflecting fragmentation of resource discovery across both generational and platform-based boundaries.
  • They articulate a gap: there is no one-stop shop or directory for all activities, making it difficult for activists without the right digital connections to get involved or stay informed.

Barriers, Fragmentation, and Centralisation

  • The current state is described as "scattered" — WhatsApp, email, Teams, and printing all serve different groups and activities, and less tech-literate members can be "lost" amid the spread.
  • Student peers navigate real-time digital channels, while local groups assemble with printouts and monthly meetings, highlighting a substantial cultural and generational divide in organising practice.
  • Advocates for a unified, categorised online platform accommodating both fast-paced digital engagement and more traditional, offline participation, with clear segmentation to prevent topic overload or confusion.

Reflective and Empathetic Advocacy

  • As the youngest participant in the sample, Participant 6 is strikingly empathetic to the participation needs of older activists, advocating strongly for features benefiting those with minimal digital fluency.
  • Their experiences highlight pragmatic intersections between digital-native habits and structural community needs — pushing for a system that bridges technical gaps and actively values everyone's input, regardless of digital comfort level.
  • The interview concludes with advocacy for systems that allow diverse, interconnected engagement, improving not only campaign reach but the sense of community across generations and activist backgrounds.
Interview 5

Participant 5

Journey into Activism and Early Community

  • Participant 5's entry to Amnesty International was motivated by both social conscience (prompted by a poster about women tortured in Indonesia) and a wish to meet likeminded people in a new city.
  • Their earliest involvement was strongly shaped by personal connections — receiving informal support and mentoring from an established member, which exemplified a welcoming culture in local groups.
  • Their progression from member to case holder, country coordinator and then North Africa team leader reflects the capacity for volunteers to develop skills and take on new roles as their engagement deepens.

Digital Adoption and the Spectrum of Digital Literacy

  • They describe themselves as "conversant with online stuff", managing Facebook and Instagram for teams, but note a pronounced gap — many older members have little or no IT confidence, and few use social media.
  • The lowest common denominator for group communications is email; only recently has the need for hard copy communications diminished. For some years, hard copies went out to those in their 90s to avoid exclusion.
  • Even amongst older activists, there is variation: some are quite IT literate, others are not. The challenge is not simply generational but also about attitudes and exposure to technology.

Communication Channels and Multi-Modal Approach

  • The monthly groups newsletter is highly valued, especially by those in more traditional roles (e.g., group chairs and country coordinators), as it provides timely, actionable information.
  • Physical magazines and leaflets are seen as irreplaceable by many, not just for accessibility, but as tangible conversation tools at stalls or events; a printed resource can be handed directly to someone, sparking engagement in a way that a website link or email often cannot.
  • Within groups, WhatsApp subgroups are used for urgent or time-sensitive requests, while Facebook is preferred for campaign storytelling and adapting content from partners or the International Secretariat.

Moderation and Safety in Online Spaces

  • They have found Facebook's moderation tools are largely absent or ineffective, especially on topics such as Palestine or LGBTI refugees, where hateful or toxic responses are common when posts attract a wider audience.
  • Experiences of targeted abuse and "walls of hate" from anonymous accounts highlight the risks of promoting posts outside trusted networks; manual deletion is sometimes the only recourse.
  • Attempts at reasoned engagement with hostile commenters are rarely productive; such individuals often show no interest in dialogue and may not even be genuine local actors.

Security, Privacy, and Exposure

  • Amnesty activism itself has not led to direct safety threats for them, but trustee work at another charity resulted in their address being stolen and disturbing threats by post, highlighting the risks of public-facing activism.
  • A former professional role resulted in their home details being found online, leading to a frightening phone call — underscoring difficulties in fully protecting privacy for those handling sensitive work.
  • They are aware that some Amnesty staff and activists use pseudonyms, particularly those working in their country of origin, yet this is not widespread across the wider member base.

Cross-Platform and Resource Fragmentation

  • They run or contribute to multiple digital channels (Facebook, Instagram, X, WhatsApp), but also articulate an ethical tension: some platforms, while problematic, have existing audiences that are hard to leave behind.
  • The international structure of Amnesty means information is fragmented: campaign materials, case documents, and action resources are scattered across multiple drives, websites, and closed systems.
  • This fragmentation, combined with mediocre search tools on the main website, often makes it impossible for even experienced volunteers to locate the right resources without asking others directly.

Inclusion, Diversity, and Organisational Change

  • They feel there has been visible improvement in Amnesty's approach to inclusion, especially after an internal review — this is reflected both in campaign focus and in more diverse visual representation.
  • Despite these organisational efforts, long-serving and less digitally confident members often report feeling forgotten, particularly when preferred communication methods (like printed case profiles) are withdrawn.
  • They emphasise that digital exclusion is not merely about age: within every age cohort, there are individuals who are highly literate with technology, and others who are not — tailored support and multi-modal engagement are necessary.

Community Building and Mentorship

  • The informal "taking under the wing" by experienced activists in earlier years was a vital part of their integration, and they see value in replicating this kind of support (online or offline) to welcome new members.
  • They believe online onboarding alone is insufficient for building strong attachments to the cause or organisation, particularly for those in rural or dispersed areas; a sense of belonging grows best through active relationships and direct support.
  • They express concern that new platforms should not lose sight of these relational aspects, even as they seek to improve discoverability and engagement through digital means.

Platform Features, Storytelling, and Network Value

  • Facebook is particularly valued for longer-form storytelling and the ability to run serial social media efforts (posting elements of a campaign over several days for greater impact and reach).
  • The use of images and shareable content is critical for urgent actions; teams regularly adapt and distribute posts from the International Secretariat, Freedom House, and other partners, but this depends on having easy access to the right material.
  • WhatsApp is used selectively for immediate action, but it is not open or browsable — groups remain siloed, and broader peer learning is hindered as a result.

Fragmentation, Discoverability, and a Case for Centralisation

  • They see existing knowledge, actions, and resources as being "all over the place" — current systems are daunting for new or less confident members, and the closed nature of most channels makes intergroup learning inefficient and serendipitous.
  • The Amnesty website's search function is a particular source of frustration, often yielding nothing useful or overwhelming results, reinforcing the need for a more discoverable, user-friendly hub.
  • There is a clear appetite for a centralised community platform that supports both digital and non-digital members, enables browsing of other groups' work, and breaks down existing organisational silos.

Respect for Legacy Members and Hybrid Solutions

  • Their main concern is for older participants, emphasising that many founders and stalwarts of the movement remain actively involved but do not wish to move online; making online engagement the only method would risk losing vital institutional knowledge and support.
  • They advocate for ongoing provision of both digital and physical materials to accommodate different user needs, pointing to differences even within the same generation.
  • Ultimately, they argue for a hybrid, flexible platform approach that values both the past and present: accessible, supportive, and able to adapt to the diverse realities of today's activist community.
Interview 4

Participant 4

Personal and Activist Background

  • Participant 4 is an author of historical fiction and an active volunteer with a regional Amnesty International group, where they report on particular world regions and help manage aspects of social media.
  • They were drawn to direct activism out of frustration with passive concern, particularly during the rise of the refugee crisis and negative rhetoric about refugees; their involvement is relatively recent (about two and a half years).
  • Their prior charity work was largely animal-focused, but they now engage in a broad range of human rights activities, attending monthly meetings and events with a strong sense of community responsibility.

Digital Presence and Community Involvement

  • They maintain distinct personal, professional, and activist social media presences, participating in various overlapping online communities, including book-focused spaces and Amnesty-related groups.
  • Although they express ambivalence about certain platforms (such as Facebook), they remain because of its unique capacity to connect them with a dispersed personal and professional network.
  • Their activist role involves amplifying both local and international Amnesty messaging, sometimes acting as a bridge between different groups and platforms.

Social Media Practices and Strategies

  • For campaign work, they frequently share, like, and repost group and Amnesty content across networks. They value the ability to share content between platforms, especially for visual-based campaigns.
  • They regularly translate and repost messages from international groups, using translation features to navigate diverse content and maintain global solidarity.
  • Event promotion and resource sharing are core activities, with attention to the unique affordances of each platform.

Moderation, Abuse, and Platform Safety

  • They have not had personal posts removed, but are aware of group-level content takedowns, particularly through automated keyword flagging. Colleagues have occasionally contested such removals, with variable outcomes.
  • Negative and hostile responses, especially around women's rights, are common; their approach is calm engagement unless comments appear conspiratorial or abusive, in which case they disengage.
  • A single incident — being tracked down at home — highlighted the importance of privacy, though they are careful to avoid linking activist accounts to personal identity and advocate for pseudonymity when necessary.

Platform Limitations and Need for Nuanced Moderation

  • Automated moderation is seen as often overzealous, with crucial discussions about violence or human rights sometimes suppressed; they point to the need for context-sensitive moderation.
  • Facebook and Twitter are perceived as having deprioritised robust moderation and user protection, sometimes encouraging outrage for engagement. This discourages open conversations on sensitive topics.
  • BlueSky is highlighted as offering a healthier user environment, responsive moderation, and a better process for following up on abuse reports, though even here action can be slow.

Accessibility, Diversity, and Exclusion

  • They are thoughtful about their own perspective and advocate engaging directly affected users meaningfully in platform design.
  • Witnessing dismissals of the ongoing reality of sexism and racism is common, and they find it notable when individuals deny these experiences online.
  • There is a clear desire for platforms to protect vulnerable users proactively, rather than relying on confrontation or expecting individuals to manage exclusion or abuse alone.

Event Organisation, Group Communication, and Tools

  • WhatsApp and email are the primary tools for group organisation: WhatsApp for quick, time-sensitive updates, and email for longer communications, event planning, and distributing minutes.
  • They outline several recurring group events (book and cake sales, musical evenings, letter-writing campaigns, street collections, and attending marches), coordinated mainly through internal digital channels.
  • They suggest that alternative tools such as Signal may offer greater privacy and ethical reassurance, though WhatsApp remains more widely adopted within the group.

Regional Isolation and the Promise of Centralisation

  • There is a sense of regional isolation: it often feels like each local group operates independently and must proactively seek out others, with limited visibility of wider Amnesty activity.
  • Current siloed communication (WhatsApp, email) hinders easy discovery of collaborations, sharing of event ideas, or cross-regional support.
  • A centralised, discoverable community platform could help groups connect, share resources and templates, and feel less isolated — facilitating not only functional coordination, but a stronger collective identity.

Discoverability, Resource Sharing, and Peer Learning

  • They experience friction when trying to reach out to other groups — searching across platforms and followers for contacts is time-consuming, limiting the organic spread of successful initiatives.
  • They see great value in being able to "copy and remix" resources, templates, and event formats from other groups, both regionally and internationally.
  • The move towards a central, open space is seen as a solution: enabling discoverability, mutual learning, and a practical sense of connection beyond closed apps and private channels.

Motivation for Platform Change

  • Participant 4 expresses strong support for a platform offering both open discussion and secure, private group chats, adaptable for the needs of different kinds of activism and levels of risk.
  • They advocate nudging users towards better privacy practices through recommendations, rather than mandates, and see benefit in co-existing advice for using third-party tools responsibly.
  • The overall hope is to help activists feel better supported, more visible, and capable of drawing on a wider range of peer experience — moving "from isolated group to collective movement".
Interview 3

Participant 3

Overarching Themes

  • Groups and networks rely heavily on centralised information and direction from Amnesty.
  • Communication gaps and unclear explanations from the centre regularly cause frustration for activists.
  • Digital skills and technology use vary widely, skewing older and favouring email and WhatsApp; training and support are needed for broader adoption.
  • Barriers exist to connecting with Amnesty members outside of local groups; outreach is bottlenecked by central office processes.
  • Peer sharing and best practice exchange mostly occur in person; online equivalents are lacking.
  • New technology will only help if its benefits are tangible, well-communicated, and supported by robust onboarding and guidance.
  • Technology isn't a cure-all; face-to-face, informal activism remains important.

Background and Role

  • Board member and Vice Chair at Amnesty UK, chairing the "Building a Powerful Movement" subcommittee.
  • Over 30 years' experience as an Amnesty activist, chair, and treasurer in various local groups.
  • Role is purely voluntary and elected by activists.

Information and Support

  • Amnesty is recognised for its research, information sharing, and general support to local groups and thematic networks.
  • Local groups and networks typically work independently but rely on Amnesty for campaign direction, urgent action alerts, and guidance.
  • The structure includes both geographically-based local groups and thematic networks, which often function in silos.

Community Engagement and Personal Networks

  • There is frequent overlap between group members who participate in other campaigns or organisations (e.g. Palestine Solidarity Campaign, Greenpeace), with information shared informally.
  • Local group activism is highly relational and sometimes information flows through individuals with multiple memberships.
  • In-person meetings remain the mainstay of local engagement and information flow, with group actions decided collectively.

Gaps and Issues with Centralisation

  • Support and practical guidance for groups (e.g. on school outreach, campaign methods) are often requested but not systematically provided.
  • Amnesty's website is seen as hard to navigate, with search results often irrelevant.
  • When central staff leave or programmes end (e.g. urgent action schemes, school speaker programme), activists find themselves with outdated information and no clear explanation. This leads to actions being taken on obsolete issues and a sense that "the system gets in the way."
  • Sudden disappearance of initiatives, or projects slowly drifting away without clear communication, undermines trust and engagement.

Technology and Digital Platforms

  • Most local groups rely on email for organisation and communication; WhatsApp use is growing but is still new for many.
  • Facebook pages exist but are used inconsistently, often managed by the one member with more digital confidence.
  • Exploration of new digital platforms (BlueSky, TikTok, Instagram) is largely limited to the most tech-savvy member.
  • Email newsletters are appreciated by older and retired members but are acknowledged as less suitable for younger audiences.
  • Many activists are apprehensive about using new technologies, especially those with reputational or generational barriers (e.g. scepticism about TikTok).

Barriers to Outreach and Networking

  • Local groups cannot directly contact Amnesty members in their area unless they are already group members; outreach must happen via central office and scheduled comms.
  • This bottleneck makes event promotion and local mobilisation less effective and more time-consuming.
  • There is a need for platforms that allow direct, locality-based outreach and connection among activists.

Peer Learning and Best Practice Sharing

  • In-person events (such as regional or national conferences) occasionally feature "We Did This" segments for sharing successful (or failed) activism stories and practical tips.
  • A monthly email newsletter from Amnesty exists for groups to share achievements and updates, but recognises it mainly serves people already engaged via email and with time to read it.
  • There is no online space for regular, cross-group peer support, learning, or real-time sharing of tips and "what works".

Openness to Change and the Limits of Technology

  • While Participant 3 is moderately tech-savvy and open to new platforms, they note that some members — especially older activists — are content with long-standing practices and are unlikely to change, regardless of training.
  • Broader Amnesty community includes many activists outside local groups who would benefit from a digital community platform, especially for digital-first campaigning and interaction.
  • Technology is not viewed as a cure-all; successful community engagement will require balancing online platforms with support for in-person and informal activity.

Recommendations and Platform Hopes

  • Provide clear, tangible benefits: the new platform must solve real-world problems for average members, not just offer abstract improvements.
  • Enable members to:
    • Broadcast event invitations and requests for help or tips to local and national activists.
    • Find and contact local Amnesty members not already in a group.
    • Collaborate more easily across groups and regions, with quick responses to local developments (e.g. policy changes).
    • Access up-to-date campaign information and resources in a user-friendly way.
  • Training, support, and a proactive onboarding campaign will be crucial for adoption — otherwise, the platform risks being ignored or seen as "for other people."
  • Suggests involving both tech-savvy and less tech-confident activists in platform development and user testing to ensure relevance and accessibility.
Interview 2

Participant 2

Overarching Themes

  • Strong one-to-one relationships, but limited cross-community connection.
  • Staff-centric processes restrict community autonomy and organic growth.
  • Platforms must fit users' existing habits and daily routines for engagement.
  • Siloed groups hinder collaboration and knowledge-sharing.
  • Sustainable online communities require both active facilitation and simple, accessible design.

Background and Role

  • Participant 2 is the Human Rights Education Team Manager at Amnesty International UK. Their remit includes direct projects delivered by their team (working with teachers, activists, creatives) and shaping human rights education methodology organisation-wide.
  • They have a decade's experience at Amnesty, having previously job-shared and then returned from maternity leave.
  • Their route to Amnesty was via a payroll donation option at a first job aged 16, which led to attending a local conference and some group activity.

Community Engagement: Strengths and Challenges

  • Strength: AIUK is effective at building and maintaining strong, long-term individual relationships with activists and members — described as "really, really strong" relationships that develop over time and can be deeply supportive.
  • Challenge — staff-centric structures: Many opportunities or community activities feel like they must pass through staff, which can stifle organic development and autonomy. Activism should not depend so heavily on the office or staff facilitators.
  • Challenge — siloed engagement: People are often connected only within their entry point (e.g., community fundraising, teachers' network) and rarely introduced to other networks or roles, limiting cross-pollination.

Online Community Experiences (External)

  • Member of a Facebook group focused on postnatal health — describes it as genuinely supportive, "real", and a space for sharing both experiences and self-advocacy.
  • Participates in a climate justice WhatsApp group — finds WhatsApp effective because notifications keep the group top-of-mind and easily accessible in a busy, family-centred life.
  • Notes the benefits over email-based groups, which often end up ignored or lost in spam folders.

Online Community Experiences (Professional / Amnesty-Related)

  • Managed online learning and community activities for educators — former platforms were purpose-built or adopted (e.g., a dedicated educator platform, FutureLearn for online courses).
  • These platforms were most useful for resource sharing and focused short-term peer support, especially for teachers working on similar campaign or curriculum topics.
  • Engagement dipped without sustained staff facilitation or clear, recurring value. WhatsApp emerged as the default solution for small group communication when the platform did not facilitate connection.

Moderation Experience and Perspectives

  • Managed courses on FutureLearn which attracted some trolling or targeted negativity after news events. Most "moderation" was light-touch: staff might nudge participants privately if a post was off-topic.
  • Recognises the sector-wide tension between robust moderation for safety and upholding freedom of expression.
  • Striking the balance between facilitating critique (desired for learning) and pre-empting unsafe or disruptive speech is a persistent challenge that will need careful policy and guidance.

Security and Privacy Awareness

  • Has not personally experienced online targeting or doxxing, partly due to a back-office, non-public-facing role.
  • Colleagues and contacts have experienced visa denials due to activism, being targeted online by both the public and hostile states, and genuine fear of surveillance — particularly among frontline activists and those engaging with state-critical campaigns.
  • Their primary role in such situations has been to signpost affected staff/activists to support resources.

Platform Preferences, Features, and Functionality

  • WhatsApp and "platforms where people already are" (e.g., Facebook for teachers) are effective due to convenience and existing familiarity.
  • New platforms face adoption challenges if they require users to "remember to go" somewhere else.
  • Platforms used for children's nurseries provide a good example: all updates, health information, and records in one place — intuitive and consolidating.

Past Amnesty Platform Experiences

  • Custom educator platform (significant budget investment):
    • Built as an add-on to the Amnesty site with single sign-on.
    • Functionality and user interface were underwhelming for the cost and ambition.
    • Weak notifications: either users received everything (spam) or nothing (no engagement reminder).
    • Lacked mechanisms for ownership and self-sustaining use; required staff to orchestrate participation.
    • Resource sharing worked well; policy and copyright hurdles for website publication were bypassed via the platform.
  • NationBuilder pilot for community organising:
    • Allowed geographic segmentation and constituency-based grouping, helpful for matching speakers to schools or local groups.
    • Previous functionality allowed external parties (e.g., schools) to find speakers by postcode and request engagements, mediating privacy by not exposing activists' contact details.
    • Current lack of such a tool is a major gap: requests for speakers often cannot be fulfilled, especially outside of London.

Connecting, Matching, and Mapping Needs

  • There is no longer a system to map requests to local activists, speakers, or trainers. Previously, a database of vetted volunteer speakers (with criminal checks, references, safeguarding, and training) enabled community connection.
  • The platform allowed for mapping who was most active (circles of commitment) so the most engaged could be invited to train or support others. This resource is currently lost.
  • Requests from schools or events often have to be declined without this capability.

Professional and Institutional Engagements

  • Highlights the importance of connecting groups such as professionals (lawyers, teachers, creatives) and institutions (cultural, educational) who engage through their organisational roles.
  • These groups, though not always "activists" in the traditional volunteer sense, would benefit from online spaces to share practice, skills, and connect with others working towards rights-based objectives.

Accessibility and Usability

  • Emphasises the need for platforms to be simple, inclusive, and accessible for highly skilled but less tech-confident members, particularly those recently retired.
  • The aim is to connect experienced professionals with younger activists, so design must not alienate either group.
  • Favourable towards multi-modal engagement: some will interact primarily via email, others will use browser platforms or apps; systems should be flexible enough to accommodate both.

Desired Features and Best Practice Recommendations

  • Prioritise connecting people with shared interests beyond their initial entry point, e.g., by campaign theme, local constituency, or professional background.
  • The platform must avoid funnelling everything through "the office" — organisers should support rather than gatekeep or control community connections.
  • Resource sharing and knowledge exchange features are essential for teachers, trainers, and other knowledge-focused roles.
  • Improved notification management and integrated reminders are needed to prevent both spam and user disengagement.
  • Interest in recognition and learning "pathways" for activists, supporting ongoing engagement and a sense of progression after training — envisioned as a way to transition from staff-led training towards a genuine, self-sustaining community of practice.
  • Clear private/public area separation, with certain forums or resources member-only and others open/searchable.
  • Robust onboarding and vetting processes if individuals are representing AIUK in public or educational roles, with proportionate checks for safeguarding.

Future Opportunities and Reflections

  • Recognises the activist life cycle: people's level of involvement fluctuates through student life, working/family years, and retirement. Successful community infrastructure accommodates and welcomes re-engagement at different life stages.
  • Sees strong potential in using an online platform to build bridges — linking people with different skills, backgrounds, and energies, such as retired professionals and new campaigners.
  • Emphasises that platform success depends on human facilitation and active engagement strategies, not just technical features.
Interview 1

Participant 1

Overarching Themes

  • Community technology and support at Amnesty must reflect, not override, the real patterns of engagement, digital capabilities, and risks faced by activists and staff.
  • Everyone's safety and ability to participate depend on a platform that centres privacy, simple moderation, user-driven ownership, and accessible design.
  • The balance of autonomy and accountability is critical: staff's role is enabling and supporting, not controlling.
  • Empirical lessons from past platform use — whether WhatsApp, Facebook, or specialist apps — should shape decision-making, rather than assumptions about what activists want or need.

Background and Activist Experience

  • Participant 1 is the community organiser for networks and coordinators at Amnesty International UK, supporting about 60 activists focused on specific countries/regions, as well as the anti-racism network.
  • Their background spans school-based Amnesty youth groups, university-level direct action, housing co-ops, national co-op organising, NUS Disabled Students Officer roles, and professional experience in the Disabled People's Movement, especially around social care and pandemic-era policy.
  • Their activist practice is rooted in both online and in-person organising, with a strong focus on building autonomous, resilient, and inclusive communities.

Community Entry Points and Structure

  • Amnesty International UK offers a remarkable variety of engagement routes: geographically based groups (local and youth), thematic "networks" (country/region-specific, anti-racism, feminism), and rights-holder-led groups that ensure authentic leadership — e.g., majority racialised leadership in the anti-racism network to avoid tokenism.
  • The structure is highly non-hierarchical and flexible: people may start in one group and move sideways into others based on interests, specialisation, or personal development, with no rigid "ladder" of advancement.

Technical Platform Barriers

  • The AIUK website is described as deeply problematic: designed almost exclusively for staff-led campaign activity, with activist-driven content nearly impossible to find unless you have a direct link.
  • Lack of self-publishing tools forces activists to go through staff, creating delays and bottlenecks.
  • Many activists are older or have only basic IT literacy; the current design is described as needing "an orienteering degree" to use, especially for non-digital natives.
  • Staff often have to upload blogs for activists, leading to unnecessary workload and exposure (see privacy section).

Platform Familiarity, Simplicity, and User Preferences

  • Participant 1 personally prefers simple, direct tools:
    • WhatsApp: Used for both open and "announcements-only" groups; valued for simplicity and clarity.
    • Loomio: Favoured in previous student co-op activism as an effective, easier-to-use alternative to Slack; they find Slack confusing and unfriendly.
    • MoreYoga app: An example of ideal usability — only a few clear tabs, localised/class-filtered, no unnecessary features, "everything on the screen."
  • Most of their digital community experience is pragmatically in-person or through direct messaging rather than forum-based platforms.
  • They stress the importance of avoiding assumptions about activists' digital fluency: not only older members but also younger or staff members may not engage with complex forums or tools.

Moderation and Group Management

  • Real-life moderation experience is typically informal: clear joining questions are used to maintain relevance in group membership.
  • When conflict arises (e.g., transphobic comments), their approach is to restate the group's values publicly, warn the individual privately, and remove them if needed.
  • The best-run groups use mandatory post templates (especially for accessibility info) and have rules clearly pinned and surfaced on entry.
  • Volunteer moderation has significant limitations: shortage of moderators, slow response times (sometimes 12 hours), and the volunteer nature of moderation lead to unresolved issues and hostile interactions.

Security and Privacy

  • Security risks are real and multifaceted: historical and recent examples of university surveillance, police illegally sharing protestor data, and activists being suspended as a result.
  • Experience of targeted harassment, doxxing, and death threats by far-right individuals, including attempts to find home addresses and contacting employers.
  • To mitigate risk, activists often use pseudonyms in public campaigning and on digital platforms.
  • They advocate for features that allow posting content anonymously on the front end (visible to the community) while being tracked by staff on the back end. This allows accountability for problematic posts but shields activists from unnecessary public exposure.

Activist Autonomy, Ownership, and Staff Role

  • The platform should allow the organic creation of subgroups and channels (comparable to subreddit or WhatsApp group creation) without staff intervention.
  • Staff should enable, not micromanage, platform development. Activists are often more knowledgeable about their specialisms and needs, and central oversight is neither practical nor empowering.
  • "Development is best done without me" — ownership and responsibility for the platform and its content should rest with the activist community.
  • There is simply no capacity or justification for staff to oversee every space or group, especially with 80+ activists and varied topics.

Learning from Platform Experiments

  • WhatsApp group management provided practical lessons: open groups quickly became overwhelmed with off-topic content; creating "announcements-only" groups proved much more effective and welcomed by members who required concise, relevant updates.
  • Social media campaigning during the pandemic revealed effective digital engagement strategies: use of petitions as data-gathering tools created a cascading, stepped involvement pathway — petition signers → MP emailers → meeting attendees → core campaigners.
  • This strategy not only helped core campaigns but also brought in new activists and supported disabled participation.

Accessibility and Inclusion

  • Accessibility is not just about formal audits: many visual, motion-heavy, or video-based platforms are not usable for people who rely on screen readers or keyboard-only navigation.
  • Functionality and user-friendliness outweigh flashy design: platforms are tools, not ornaments.
  • Systems must serve those least comfortable with technology. Practical accessibility: templates that require accessibility information ensure all posts are actionable by people with different needs.

Platform Recommendations

  • The ideal community platform should:
    • Be simple, intuitive, and not assume prior digital literacy.
    • Support activist-driven content creation (blogs, posts) without staff bottlenecks.
    • Allow organic subgroup/channel creation by activists themselves.
    • Include robust, clear moderation tools and require paid moderation support for official spaces to avoid volunteer burnout.
    • Make anonymous posting possible, with back-end tracking for accountability.
    • Prioritise template-based posting (e.g., for important information like accessibility).
    • Avoid over-designed, video- or image-heavy interfaces that reduce accessibility.
