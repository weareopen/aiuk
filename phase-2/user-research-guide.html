User Research and
Platform Testing Guide

The definitive guide to how the project gathered insights from the AIUK activist community — covering research methods, recruitment, ethical considerations, and a structured approach to selecting the right platform.

About this document

This document supersedes and replaces all previous documents relating to user research on the AIUK Community Platform project. It sets out the full approach taken in Phase Two — from defining what we mean by "user research" and "platform testing" through to the specific methods, ethical safeguards, and analysis frameworks used.

The goal of the research phase was to ensure that the platform ultimately selected would genuinely serve the needs of AIUK's activist community — not just the most vocal or visible members of it, but the full diversity of people involved in Amnesty's work across the UK.

User Research

Gathering insights into how individuals interact with a community, product or service — focusing on behaviours, preferences, and challenges — to identify user needs and expectations.

🙋

Platform Testing

Evaluating potential platforms by observing real users as they complete specific tasks — assessing usability, functionality, and overall user experience to find the best fit.

👥

Audiences

The groups engaged with the project, classified into primary, secondary, and tertiary tiers based on their level of involvement and how actively they would use the platform.

Audiences

Research participants were drawn from across AIUK's activist community. Not all stakeholders are users — many have an interest in the project but would not actively use the platform themselves.

User research plan

The research aimed to gather enough information to create a realistic needs analysis based on the diverse needs of the community. Five clear objectives guided the work.

Research objectives

  • Understand current collaboration practices — how activists currently collaborate with AIUK, online and offline; what tools and processes they use
  • Identify pain points — challenges activists face when engaging with AIUK or peers; inefficiencies and barriers to participation
  • Determine key platform features — essential functionalities for a new community space; what activists need to organise, share, and connect
  • Explore security and privacy requirements — especially for vulnerable individuals or those in high-risk contexts; data protection, anonymity options, and safeguarding
  • Assess technology access and digital literacies — disparities in access or skills that might hinder participation; ensuring the platform is inclusive regardless of technical expertise

Research methods

Three complementary methods were used to gather both quantitative and qualitative data:

📋

Survey

Designed and distributed online (Microsoft Forms) to primary and secondary users. Gathered needs, preferences, and experiences to identify candidates for further qualitative research.

Estimated: 100–150 respondents · 5–10 mins · 256 responses received

🎙️

Individual interviews

Semi-structured conversations with selected survey respondents. Allowed participants to expand on responses and share nuanced perspectives — especially useful for sensitive topics like security and accessibility.

6–8 interviews · 45–60 mins each · Audio recorded (with permission)

💬

Focus groups

Small groups segmented by experience level, geographic region, and type of activism. Explored collective perspectives, shared challenges, and feature priorities.

3–4 groups · 4–6 participants · 60 mins · Audio recorded (with permission)

Approach to analysis

Analysis combined quantitative and qualitative methods. Survey data was analysed through statistical summaries to identify broad trends. Focus groups and interviews generated rich qualitative data that underwent detailed thematic analysis to uncover deeper motivations and challenges.

Thematic insights were used to create persona spectrums — representing key user archetypes across axes of experience, geography, activism type, and digital literacy. These informed journey mapping exercises to visualise ideal user experiences and prioritise platform features.

Recruitment and channels

Multiple recruitment channels were used to reach participants with varying experience levels, geographic locations, and types of activism.

Recruitment channels

  • Existing databases — AIUK's databases of supporters, activists, and members; invitations via newsletters and direct email campaigns
  • Snowball sampling — working with Community Organisers and Lead Activists to identify participants beyond existing databases
  • Engaged representatives — AIUK staff identifying engaged members of regional groups, thematic committees, and other bodies

Ethical considerations

Ethical practice was central throughout the research process, particularly given the sensitive nature of activism and human rights work.

Ethical safeguards

  • Informed consent — all participants received clear information about purpose, data use, and their rights; explicit consent required before any survey, interview, or focus group
  • Data protection and confidentiality — adherence to GDPR and relevant regulations; identifying information removed or anonymised during analysis and reporting
  • Anonymity — participants could remain anonymous in surveys and pseudonymous in interviews or focus groups; aggregated responses in reporting to prevent individual identification
  • Clear boundaries — participation was entirely voluntary; participants could withdraw at any time without explanation; guidelines for respectful dialogue in focus groups
  • Protocols for sensitive information — specific care and discretion for sensitive or emotionally charged disclosures; secure storage and anonymisation of sensitive data

Diversity and inclusion

Ensuring diversity and inclusion was central to the research, reflecting AIUK's commitment to representing activists from varied backgrounds and contexts.

Inclusion commitments

  • Geographic representation — participants from different UK regions (especially Northern Ireland, Scotland, and Wales) to capture varied local contexts
  • Experience levels — from newcomers beginning their advocacy journey to seasoned professionals leading campaigns or networks
  • Language — translation support provided where needed to avoid excluding non-English-speaking participants
  • Accessibility needs — assistive technologies, plain language materials, and accommodation for participants with disabilities throughout
  • Types of activism — environmental justice, gender equality, refugee rights, anti-racism and more, reflecting the breadth of AIUK's advocacy work

Platform testing plan

The user research process delivered a needs analysis to guide the comparison of potential community platforms. Combined with desk-based research, this enabled the creation of a shortlist of platforms for hands-on testing. The plan followed a structured five-step process.

1

Synthesis

Needs analysis informed by surveys, interviews, and focus groups — identifying key features, accessibility requirements, and security considerations. Simultaneously, desk-based research explored existing community platforms and their capabilities, covering usability, reliability, scalability, support quality, integration, and cost.

2

Selection

A structured comparison matrix mapped each platform against requirements identified in the needs analysis — assessing usability, flexibility, integration, support, and cost. This produced a focused shortlist of two or three platforms to test, with trial environments configured to mirror real-world AIUK scenarios.

3

Scrutiny

Recruited users from across the activist community — reflecting diversity of role, experience, accessibility needs, and geography — tested each shortlisted platform through structured 'missions': realistic scenarios such as starting a discussion thread, uploading documents, or adjusting notification settings.

4

Sentiment

Participants completed a detailed survey rating aspects of each platform (navigation, layout, accessibility, overall satisfaction) using consistent scales, plus open-ended questions for qualitative feedback. Optional focus groups explored experiences in greater depth — probing strengths, weaknesses, and preferences.

5

Selection

All evidence — user experiences, survey results, and focus group insights — was brought together to produce a recommendation report. The report outlined each platform's strengths and weaknesses, rationale for the chosen platform, and any trade-offs. The platform selected was Discourse, hosted by Communiteq at community.amnesty.org.uk.
