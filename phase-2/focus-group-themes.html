Focus Group Themes

Significant themes drawn from focus group sessions with community organisers, youth activists, and network organisers — covering communication, platform needs, access, trust, and safeguarding.

About this document

This document captures the significant themes that emerged from focus group sessions conducted during Phase Two of the AIUK Community Platform Project. Three distinct participant groups took part: community organisers (staff), youth activists, and network organisers. Each group brought different perspectives and priorities, but many themes recurred across all three.

The findings are organised by participant group, with each group's themes numbered and supported by direct quotations from participants. Where the source document includes participant codes (e.g. B3, B6), these are preserved to maintain fidelity to the original research record.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Group A

Community Organisers (Staff)

1 Difficulties with Communication and Coordination

Tracking Engagement: Several staff mentioned the absence of a system for checking who has received information, which messages have been sent, and what interests individuals have. This leads to messy tracking and can cause duplicated or missed outreach.

"We don't know who's received what comms. We don't know when they were last in contact, what they last responded to, what issues they're most interested in. That kind of like profiling and tracking."

Limited Awareness of Colleagues' Workloads and Activities: Working in separate portfolios means organisers lack clarity about what others are doing or their ability to collaborate at particular times.

2 Platform and Tool Usage

  • Preferred Tools: There is a clear preference from activists (especially those involved in sensitive work) for encrypted apps such as Signal, over platforms like WhatsApp or Teams.
  • Fragmented Channels: Communication is often dictated by the activist audience. Longstanding local groups prefer email, while others lean towards group messaging apps. Staff have little control or oversight of conversations happening in activist-run channels.

3 Features Sought in a New Platform

Discovery and Connection: Strong support for features allowing activists and organisers to find others by interest, location, and campaign.

"It'd be really good if people could kind of maybe like, like list interests or themes on a profile... it's not possible for any of us to know all of those people and know that any 20 of them are interested in [a topic]. So if they can kind of discover themselves, like through their interest on an online platform, that'd be great."

  • Privacy Controls: Activists value the ability to remain anonymous, particularly those at risk. There is interest in anonymous posting, with the caveat that unacceptable behaviour should still be traceable by moderators.
  • Moderation: Moderation is seen as necessary. There is widespread agreement that making it the job of volunteers alone will not work; a mix of approaches is favoured.
  • Group Structure and Sub-Communities: There is consensus that having specific spaces for targeted discussion (such as by topic or campaign) helps prevent information overload in general channels.
  • Boundaries and Consent in Contact: Features like friend requests to initiate private chats and the ability to block or mute are acknowledged as helpful for setting boundaries and safety.

4 Access and Inclusion

  • Generational Differences: Older activists tend to rely on email, and may need additional support to use a new platform, while younger activists are confident but may be put off by overbearing behaviour from other generations.
  • Accessibility Needs: A new platform ought to be accessible for people with disabilities (screen readers, captions, alt text) and considerate of those for whom English is not a first language (e.g. translation support).
  • Mobile Compatibility and Digital Poverty: Many users access information solely by mobile phone, so a mobile-friendly platform is seen as necessary.

5 Trust, Safety, and Culture

Building Trust: There is a strong desire for vetting or verification, which supports a sense of safety and trust.

"So much of these spaces are about trust. So it's making sure that there is like that vetting process built in, um, and that it's kind of like regulated in some way."

  • Moderation and Reporting: Users want a clear code of conduct, active modelling of inclusive behaviour, and quick responses to inappropriate content.
  • Guidance and Legal Safeguards: Regular reminders are seen as helpful so users know not to discuss unlawful activity on the platform.

6 Respect for Existing Networks

Non-Displacement of Existing Channels: The new platform is not expected to replace WhatsApp, Signal, Facebook groups, or email. Instead, it should complement them — particularly by enabling better discovery and sharing of resources.

7 Safeguarding and Permissions

  • Young People and Under-18s: There is an expectation that spaces for young people should have additional protections, including privacy settings and restricted visibility of some personal details.
  • Permission Structures: Different age bands might require different permissions — for example, under-16s might not be able to share a surname or precise location.

Further quotations from Group A

"I don't think… the moderation… should be completely volunteer based. I don't think it's… particularly fair. I don't think it will be effective."

"Being very mobile friendly will be really important."

"We can probably only have control up to a certain point, or even oversight or insight into the ways that activists communicate, because the vast majority... is with each other and not necessarily us."

Group B

Youth Activists

1 Difficulties with Communication and Coordination

Tracking Engagement: Participants described uncertainty about who had seen messages and who was engaged, leading to missed opportunities and confusion.

"It's hard to keep track of who's interested in what, especially when we use loads of different apps."

2 Platform and Tool Usage

Preferred Tools: There was a clear preference for privacy-focused apps, with discomfort expressed about using mainstream platforms for sensitive discussions.

"I'd rather use Signal for anything important, because it feels safer than WhatsApp or Facebook."

Participant B5

Fragmented Channels: The use of multiple platforms made communication messy and difficult to manage.

"Everyone's on different apps, so it gets messy trying to keep up."

Participant B2

3 Features Sought in a New Platform

Discovery and Connection: Participants valued features enabling them to find peers with similar interests or campaign goals, such as searchable profiles or interest tags.

"It would be cool if you could just search for people who care about the same stuff as you."

Participant B7

"If there was a way to list what you're interested in, it'd be easier to connect."

Participant B4

Privacy Controls: Anonymity was important, especially for those at risk, but there was consensus that moderation and traceability were needed for safety.

"I'd like to post anonymously, but only if moderators can step in if someone's out of order."

Participant B9

Moderation: Participants felt moderation should not be left solely to volunteers and needed to be robust.

"Moderation shouldn't just be volunteers – it needs to be proper."

Participant B1

Group Structure and Sub-Communities: There was support for creating specific spaces for targeted discussions to avoid information overload.

"Having separate groups for different topics would stop the main chat getting too busy."

Participant B8

Boundaries and Consent in Contact: The ability to block or mute others was seen as important for maintaining boundaries and safety.

"It's good to be able to block or mute people if you need to."

Participant B5

4 Access and Inclusion

Accessibility Needs: Concerns were raised about accessibility for people with disabilities and for those for whom English is not a first language.

"Translation or captions would help people who aren't confident with English."

Participant B2

Mobile Compatibility and Digital Poverty: Mobile access was considered essential, as many young people only have access to smartphones.

"It needs to work on phones, because that's all some people have."

Participant B6

5 Trust, Safety, and Culture

Building Trust: Trust and safety were seen as foundational, with participants wanting vetting or verification processes and clear codes of conduct.

"You need to know there's some kind of vetting, so people feel safe."

Participant B3

"Having a code of conduct makes it clear what's OK and what's not."

Participant B4

Moderation and Reporting: The need for swift responses to inappropriate behaviour and clear guidelines was emphasised.

6 Respect for Existing Networks

Non-Displacement of Existing Channels: The new platform should complement, not replace, established channels like WhatsApp, Signal, Facebook groups, or email.

"We're not going to stop using WhatsApp or Signal, but it'd be good to have one place to find resources."

Participant B7

Young People and Under-18s: There was an expectation of additional protections for young people, such as privacy settings and restricted sharing of personal details for under-16s.

"Younger people shouldn't have to share their real names or locations."

Group C

Network Organisers

1 Communication and Coordination

Tracking and Outreach: Participants described persistent challenges with tracking engagement.

"We don't know who's received what comms. We don't know when they were last in contact, what they last responded to, what issues they're most interested in. That kind of like profiling and tracking."

  • Outreach is often hampered by outdated contact details and uncertainty about who is being reached.
  • Communication is fragmented across email, Teams, WhatsApp, and other channels, with little oversight of activist-run groups.

Awareness of Colleagues' Activities: Working in separate portfolios leads to limited visibility of others' work.

"Working in separate portfolios means organisers lack clarity about what others are doing or their ability to collaborate at particular times."

  • Finding the right staff contact is often a struggle, despite recent improvements with dedicated coordinators for specific groups.

2 Tools and Platform Preferences

Current Practices:

Tool usage across the network

  • Email — Main outreach to local groups. Issues: outdated lists, inconsistent responses.
  • Teams — Meetings and campaign planning. Issues: not widely adopted by activists.
  • WhatsApp — Informal activist-run groups. Issues: privacy concerns, lack of guidance.
  • Discord — Considered for thematic groups (e.g. feminist network). Issues: safeguarding concerns.
  • Staff have little control over activist-run channels.
  • No official guidance exists for secure group communication.

Security and Privacy: Concerns about sharing personal data (e.g. phone numbers on WhatsApp).

"It's your phone number... it's a data concern for sure."

  • Pseudonyms are used for high-risk activists, with email aliases set up on request.
  • For some, joining certain groups is not possible due to privacy or security risks.

3 Feature Requirements for a New Platform

Discovery and Connection: Strong interest in features enabling activists to connect by interest, campaign, or location.

"It'd be really good if people could list interests or themes on a profile... discover themselves through their interest on an online platform."

  • Desire for a "meet the team" page to clarify staff roles and responsibilities.

Resource Sharing: Need for a shared space to upload presentations, onboarding materials, and campaign resources, especially for non-staff activists.

Moderation and Safeguarding:

Moderation requirements

  • Hybrid moderation — Volunteer-only moderation seen as ineffective; a professional element is needed.
  • Age-gated permissions — Protect under-18s (no surnames or location sharing).
  • Keyword blocking — Prevent accidental disclosure of sensitive information.
  • Safeguarding is a key concern, especially for under-18s and diaspora activists at risk of transnational repression.

Inclusion and Accessibility: Translation and captioning features requested for non-English speakers.

"Translation or captions would help people who aren't confident with English."

  • Platform should be mobile-friendly and accessible for people with disabilities (e.g. screen reader support).

4 Onboarding and Organisational Structure

Onboarding Gaps: No standard onboarding process exists for new activists; networks often create their own.

"We created our own onboarding process because there wasn't anything established... explaining the wider amnesty structure."

  • The Country Coordinator Forum uses a six-month induction plan, but support varies by team leader capacity.

Organisational Complexity: Amnesty's structure — covering local groups, thematic networks, student groups, staff, International Secretariat, and AIUK — is confusing for newcomers, and lack of awareness of other teams' activities hinders collaboration.

5 Security, Safeguarding, and Diaspora Challenges

High-Risk Scenarios: Transnational repression is a major concern, especially for those working on China and Hong Kong.

"Transnational repression is very real... activists delete all Amnesty-related data when returning to high-risk regions."

  • Pseudonyms and strict data minimisation are used for safety.

Platform Safeguards:

  • Calls for technical features such as keyword blocking and audit trails for moderation actions.
  • Need for robust vetting and consent processes to protect at-risk users.

6 Recommendations

Key recommendations from network organisers

  • Centralised coordination hub: Integrate tracking, interest-matching, and resource sharing to reduce duplication and improve collaboration.
  • Secure pseudonym system: Enable GDPR-compliant alias management for high-risk users.
  • Multi-language and accessible platform: Prioritise translation, captioning, and screen reader compatibility.
  • Clear onboarding and training resources: Centralise compulsory training, risk assessment templates, and key contact details.
  • Facilitate connections across networks and with international Amnesty sections: Support best practice sharing and collaboration.
