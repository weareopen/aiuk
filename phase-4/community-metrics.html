Community Metrics

A framework for measuring the health, growth, and maturity of the AIUK online community — combining quantitative platform analytics with qualitative sense-of-community measurement.

About this document

The AIUK community is made up of a wide and varied audience — dedicated and thoughtful activists, groups, and networks. With a new community manager in place and a shortlist of community platforms to consider, this document sets out how to think about metrics for the evolving online community.

These metrics should not be thought of in the same way as corporate KPIs. Rather, they are indicators of growth. Communities grow and mature in different ways, but there are commonalities which become visible when enough data is collected and analysed over time.

"Data is the single best asset you have to develop a thriving community… But you need to use it correctly. You need to know what data to collect, how to collect it, how to analyze it, and how to use that data to identify future actions you should take."

Richard Millington, Buzzing Communities

Community management is a proactive, future-focused activity. Nevertheless, it involves some reactive elements due to the unpredictable nature of human interaction — especially when mediated by technology. This document combines experience in community development with the specific framework laid out in Buzzing Communities, which remains a foundation of sound advice many years after publication.

Community Lifecycle Model

The AIUK online community is expected to progress through four distinct stages of development, each characterised by different patterns of growth, activity, and community connection. In practice, these stages may overlap or vary — specialist groups based on region or interest already exist and may be at different points in the cycle simultaneously.

Stage 1

Inception

0–3 months · Pilot started Jan 26, 2026

The community manager's role is crucial. Research shows 50–100% direct growth via management effort, with 0–50% member-initiated activity. Members experience only a small sense of community as relationships are just beginning to form.

Stage 2

Establishment

3–12 months

Communities transition to 50–90% referral growth. Member-initiated activity rises to 50–90% of total. Sense of Community scores range 0–24 (McMillan & Chavis). Self-sustaining behaviour begins to emerge.

Stage 3

Maturity

1–3 years

90%+ referral growth. 90–99% member-initiated activity. Sense of Community score reaches 24–72, reflecting strong member connections and shared identity. Communities become largely self-governing.

Stage 4

Mitosis

Variable timeline

Mature communities subdivide into specialised groups. Activity: 50–99% member-initiated. Sense of Community scores reach 72–96. Growth patterns become variable as communities fragment into focused subgroups.

Sense of Community — McMillan & Chavis Framework

Sense of Community scoring is based on the work of McMillan and Chavis (1986) and comprises four key factors:

  • Membership — emotional security and belonging
  • Influence — mutual impact between individual and community
  • Integration and fulfilment of needs — meeting physical and psychological needs
  • Shared emotional connection — positive affect and shared history

Community Conversion Funnel

The chosen community platform should enable member progression to be tracked through five distinct engagement levels, measuring conversion rates at each stage. This funnel model makes it possible to understand where people drop off and what interventions might help them move to the next level of engagement.

1

Visitors — Discovery Mode

Initial traffic to the platform, typically driven by outreach campaigns and promotional activities.

2

Registered Members — Intend to Engage

Completed the sign-up process but may not yet have participated in discussions or activities.

3

Participants — Taken Action

Made their first contribution to discussions or activities on the platform.

4

Regulars — Sustain Action

Demonstrate consistent engagement patterns over extended periods of time.

5

Volunteers — Become Activists

Take on advanced responsibilities and leadership roles within the community.

Quantitative Metrics

Most modern community platforms provide built-in analytics through an admin dashboard that can capture the majority of the metrics below without requiring external tools or database queries. These native reports prioritise user privacy and operate entirely within the platform's own systems.

Discourse is used as a reference throughout this section, as it has the most comprehensive reporting features of the platforms on the shortlist. The admin dashboard reports are accessible via /admin/dashboard/reports and provide over 40 different metrics covering user engagement, content performance, and community health.

1a. Traffic and Growth Metrics

The purpose of measuring traffic and growth metrics is to ensure that the community is on a healthy trajectory.

Metric Discourse Report Notes
Unique visits per traffic source, comparing current performance against previous 30 days Consolidated Pageviews (or Site Traffic) Consolidated Pageviews shows pageviews by logged-in users, anonymous visitors, and crawlers. Top Traffic Sources lists external referrers.
Registrations per traffic source — insight into conversion trends Signups & Top Traffic Sources Signups shows new registrations; combine with Top Traffic Sources referrer list to attribute sources.
Conversion rates from visitors to registered members (derived manually) Calculate ratio: Signups ÷ Consolidated Pageviews for the same period.

1b. Activity and Participation Metrics

Measuring activity and participation enables the community team to see how people are engaging with the AIUK mission and with one another.

Metric Discourse Report Notes
Total number of posts per month Posts Posts report shows count of new posts in the selected period.
Average number of posts per member (derived manually) Posts ÷ Daily Engaged Users (or New Contributors) over the time period.
Total number of discussions initiated per month Topics Topics report shows new topics created in the selected period.
Number of participating members Daily Engaged Users or New Contributors Daily Engaged Users counts users who posted or liked. New Contributors counts those making their first post.

1c. Community Health Metrics

Measuring the health of the community is important for understanding which elements are attractive to members and which may be causing friction or disengagement.

Metric Discourse Report Notes
Number of conflicts per month Flags Flags report shows new flag actions raised by members or staff.
Number of antagonistic members Top Ignored / Muted Users Lists users who have been muted or ignored by others in the community.
Average number of visits per member (30 days) User Visits User Visits report shows daily and monthly active users.

Relationship Building Metrics

These metrics can be captured quarterly using Discourse's native features. They focus on the quality of connections being formed, rather than raw volume of activity.

2a. Initial Engagement Indicators

Understanding how new members get started in the community is key to helping them develop a sense of belonging.

Metric Discourse Feature or Report Notes
Number of members who approach others for initial dialogue Profile Visits View "Profile Visits" in user profiles to see who visits whom.
Number of members who respond to initial discussions Time to First Response Time to First Response shows average hours until the first reply to a new topic.
Number of discussions advanced to self-disclosure Likes & Reply Chains Use the Likes report for positive responses; inspect topic views to gauge conversation depth.

2b. Ongoing Relationship Metrics

Once community members are established, it is important to understand what keeps them engaged and connected over time.

Metric Discourse Feature Notes
Number of members who participated in joint activities Badge Awards Badges report shows awarded badges. Create a "joint activity" badge to track collaborative participation.
Number of members maintaining ongoing relationships Profile Visits & Likes Combine profile-visit counts with likes given and received to identify sustained peer connections.
Familiarity with other members (survey) Custom User Fields Use built-in Staff User Fields to collect survey responses; view results via /admin/customize/user_fields.

Leadership Development Metrics

Achieving community maturity requires distributed leadership through a network of volunteers. Tracking the growth of volunteer roles and their relationship to broader community health is essential for long-term sustainability.

Metric Discourse Report Notes
Number of members in insider group (volunteers) Group Members /groups/{group_name}/members lists all volunteers in a given group.
Regular members transitioning to volunteer roles Trust Level Growth & Group Members Trust Level Growth shows promotions; check group membership lists for role changes.
Correlation between volunteer numbers and sense of community (derived manually) Correlate volunteer count (Group Members) with survey scores from Custom User Fields.
Correlation between volunteer numbers and growth (derived manually) Compare volunteer count (Group Members) with Signups or Consolidated Pageviews over the same period.

Why volunteer numbers matter

The presence of volunteers is one of the strongest indicators of a healthy, maturing community. Volunteers signal that members have moved beyond passive consumption to active ownership — a critical threshold for long-term sustainability. Tracking correlations between volunteer numbers and both sense of community scores and overall growth reveals whether volunteer-led energy is genuinely driving the community forward.

Qualitative Metrics

The concept of a 'sense of community' can be difficult to measure through quantitative data alone. Regular surveys, grounded in McMillan and Chavis's psychological sense of community framework, provide a structured approach to this challenge.

🏠

Membership

Emotional security and belonging — whether members feel they are part of something meaningful and that others share their commitment.

⚖️

Influence

The mutual impact between the individual and the community — whether members feel they can shape the community and that the community shapes them.

✅

Integration & Needs Fulfilment

Meeting physical and psychological needs — whether the community provides genuine value that justifies continued participation.

Shared Emotional Connection

The fourth dimension of McMillan and Chavis's framework — positive affect and shared history — is perhaps the most difficult to measure but the most important for long-term community health. It develops slowly through repeated positive interactions, shared experiences, and a growing sense of collective identity. Longitudinal survey questions tracking this dimension over time reveal whether the community is deepening or stagnating.

Surveys using this framework can yield quantitative data on community bond strength and member satisfaction. In addition, survey questions should capture qualitative data around:

  • Member feelings of belonging
  • Confidence in community leadership
  • Awareness of mutual benefits from participation
  • Appreciation for shared community experiences

Together, these elements provide a rounded picture of community health and sustainability — one that numbers alone cannot supply.

A note on sampling

Dependent on the size of the community, sampling techniques may be necessary to assess individual engagement patterns. Systematic sampling — such as every fifth member by join date — can provide representative data while keeping the workload manageable for the community management team.
