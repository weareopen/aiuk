Considerations for Youth Demographic

A detailed look at the legal, safeguarding, and practical challenges of including under-18s on the AIUK community platform — and a recommendation for how to proceed.

About this document

Young people under 18 years of age are important to AIUK's activism. The significant challenge in this area is the implementation of the UK's new Online Safety Act, which comes into effect in July 2025.

This document examines the legal obligations, examples from other organisations, promising practices, available options, and a recommendation for how AIUK should approach the youth demographic at launch.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

The Online Safety Act

The Act introduces requirements for platforms that may be accessed by children, with Ofcom mandating that providers must complete children's risk assessments and implement specific safety measures for users under the age of 18.

Key compliance obligations

  • Any organisation launching a new service after 16 January 2025 must carry out a children's access assessment within three months of the first day of operation, and annually thereafter.
  • An illegal content risk assessment must also be carried out within three months of starting a new service, and kept up-to-date as Ofcom updates risk profiles.
  • Potential fines for non-compliance are up to £18 million or 10% of global revenue.

Specific requirements

Platforms likely to be accessed by children must:

  • Conduct detailed children's risk assessments
  • Implement age-appropriate design features
  • Provide robust content moderation and reporting mechanisms
  • Ensure appropriate supervision and safety measures
  • Apply "safety by design" principles for all platform features

Strongest protections for under-18s

The Act adopts a risk-based approach, recognising that different platforms pose varying risks to minors. The strongest protections apply to children under 18, with platforms required to prevent access to harmful content including content relating to suicide, self-harm, eating disorders, and violent or abusive material.

What are other organisations doing?

Action for ME

Has a dedicated young person's forum which requires parental consent to join and provides clear online safety guidance.

The Mix

An online community allowing 13–25 year-olds to talk anonymously. They have a public forum with prominent support numbers, moderation, and content filtering.

Scout Association

Has developed comprehensive online safety protocols that exceed the Act's requirements, adapting their "Yellow Card" safeguarding code for digital environments.

TREVORspace

Allows users aged 13–24 to sign up to its discussion space with comprehensive Terms & Conditions, strong community guidelines, and professional moderation.

Voices of Youth (UNICEF)

Runs U-Report, a blogging platform working across messenger services (WhatsApp, Viber, Telegram) with safety measures and age-appropriate content policies.

Promising practices

Although it is very early days of the Online Safety Act, some promising practices are emerging:

Age-appropriate design

  • Private by default — setting all accounts for users under 18 to private automatically.
  • Tiered access systems — implementing different access levels based on age, with enhanced protections for younger users. This may also involve parental consent.
  • Limited data collection — minimising personal data collection and implementing strong privacy protections.

Risk assessment framework

  • Platform-specific risks — understanding how features such as direct messaging, file sharing, and group discussions could be misused.
  • Content moderation capacity — ensuring adequate resources for monitoring and responding to concerning content.
  • Incident response procedures — outlining clear protocols for handling safeguarding concerns.

Multi-layered safety measures

  • Technical controls — using automated content filtering, keyword monitoring, and some privacy-preserving user behaviour analysis.
  • Human oversight — employing trained staff who can identify and respond to safeguarding issues.
  • Clear policies — ensuring community guidelines and terms of service are age-appropriate.

Training and support systems

  • Staff training — making sure staff are up-to-date on digital safeguarding and child protection.
  • Youth education — helping young people understand online safety and digital citizenship.
  • Keeping updated — ensuring that the platform is responding to evolving online risks.

Options for the AIUK community platform

Option 1
Postpone Under-18 Access

Pros

  • Low risk
  • Simplifies initial compliance requirements

Cons

  • Delays youth participation
  • May signal that under-18 activism is not as valued

Option 2
Separate Youth Space

Pros

  • Tailored safety measures
  • Youth-led governance

Cons

  • High resource demands
  • Operational complexity

Option 3
Mixed-Age Community

Pros

  • Immediate inclusivity
  • Intergenerational dialogue

Cons

  • High safeguarding risks
  • 24/7 moderation requirement

Option 1 in detail: Postpone Under-18 Access

  • Phase 1: Launch with over-18s to focus on core functionality.
  • Phase 2: Introduce under-18 access once the community platform is more established and mature.

Option 2 in detail: Separate Youth Space

  • Dedicated platform: A separate, age-restricted space with enhanced safeguards.
  • Features: Parental consent verification, limited feature access, and real-time moderation.
  • Aligns with platforms like The Mix, which use crisis support mechanisms and age-appropriate content delivery.

Option 3 in detail: Mixed-Age Community

  • Unified space: Allow under-18s and adults to coexist with strict safeguards.
  • Safeguards: Mandatory age verification, keyword filtering, and real-time moderation.
  • Requires 24/7 moderation to protect minors from harmful content.

Recommendation: Start with over-18s

Why Option 1 is preferable

  • Compliance: The Online Safety Act is new and imposes severe penalties for non-compliance. Launching with over-18s minimises legal exposure while compliance frameworks are developed.
  • Resource Efficiency: Postponing under-18 access avoids upfront costs for age verification systems (e.g. parental consent verification, AI-driven checks) and additional staff moderation.
  • Mission preservation: Delays in youth participation are less damaging than safeguarding incidents that could undermine AIUK's reputation and credibility.
  • Phased expansion: A phased approach mirrors successful models like Action for ME, enabling gradual scaling after compliance validation.

While Options 2 and 3 offer inclusivity or efficiency, the risks of non-compliance and resource demands outweigh benefits for most NGOs. Starting with over-18s provides a stable foundation to later expand safely.

There are alternative approaches, such as combining online platforms with offline activities to reduce digital risk exposure, or working with existing youth-focused platforms that already have compliance frameworks in place. However, at this point in time, the risk is high and massively outweighs the reward.

Conclusion

Ultimately, despite providing significant challenges for organisations seeking to engage young people online, the Online Safety Act should be seen as a positive step. It is an opportunity to build safer, more productive digital spaces.

While this may require more investment in safety infrastructure and compliance monitoring, it should create safe spaces where young activists can engage meaningfully with human rights issues. Implementing safeguarding measures for young people separately from the launch of the online community platform — in a phased approach — seems the best way forward for AIUK.
