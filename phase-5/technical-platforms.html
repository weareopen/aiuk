Analysis and Comparison of Technical Platforms

A structured evaluation of 29 candidate platforms, filtered down to a shortlist of four and assessed against six categories — resulting in a clear recommendation for AIUK's community platform.

About this document

This document is structured to help WAO make a decision as to the most appropriate technical solution for AIUK's community platform. It covers the process of moving from a longlist of 29 platforms to a shortlist of four, and then applying a detailed assessment matrix to reach a recommendation.

In addition, WAO recommends that AIUK pays attention to the wider community ecosystem. Guidance on what a 'platform stack' might look like is provided in the companion document: Building an AIUK community ecosystem.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Original on Google Drive (unavailable after May 2026)

Summary

The Community Platforms Comparison spreadsheet provides a longlist of 29 platforms that WAO deemed candidates for the AIUK community platform. From this, criteria obtained from existing user stories, TBX research, and desk and user research were applied. These criteria are organised into five themes:

💬

Communication & Interaction

How effectively can members communicate, discuss, and coordinate on the platform?

👤

User Management & Identity

How are members onboarded, managed, and how do they progress through trust levels?

🔒

Safety & Compliance

Does the platform meet GDPR requirements and support safe moderation of human rights discussions?

🏅

Recognition & Motivation

Can the platform recognise different types of activism and engagement?

♿

Accessibility & Usability

Is the platform usable by all AIUK activists, including those with accessibility needs?

⚙️

Technical Constraints

Integration with Azure AD B2C SSO, data sovereignty (non-US), and sustainability requirements.

The outcome

After applying three sets of filters, four platforms remained: Discourse, Mattermost, Rocket.Chat, and Zulip. The assessment matrix resulted in a clear recommendation of Discourse as the primary platform, with the other three as strong alternative options.

Creating a shortlist

Working logically through three filters allows us to create a shortlist of platforms that meet AIUK requirements.

Filter 1: Critical Requirements

This filter removes platforms incompatible with the operational realities of a human rights NGO. The aim is to remove platforms which may pose existential risks.

Constraint 1a: Security & Privacy

Platforms were eliminated if they do not comply with GDPR (or equivalent), allow some form of self-hosting for sensitive data, or have adequate security. This removed 15 platforms — including Slack, Discord, Facebook Groups, and Hivebrite.

Constraint 1b: Budget & Sustainability

Licensing fees for some proprietary platforms exceed £20/user/month. This removed 7 platforms, including Circle.so and Mighty Networks due to their prohibitive pricing models.

Constraint 1c: Functional Alignment

Some platforms were focused on monetising communities (e.g. GroupApp, Beam.gg) or on niche use cases (e.g. LiquidFeedback for governance). These were removed as misaligned with AIUK's needs.

Filter 2: Integration Requirements

After Filter 1, seven platforms remained: Bonfire, Discourse, Element, Mattermost, Open Social, Rocket.Chat, and Zulip. Three further constraints were applied:

  • Constraint 2a: Seamless Collaboration — Bonfire, while promising in its embrace of interoperable social networking, is likely to confuse users accustomed to email. Removed.
  • Constraint 2b: SSO Integration — TBX requested Azure AD B2C integration for SSO. Platforms needed native SAML/OIDC support, not just "possible via custom development". Open Social was removed due to Drupal module dependency risk.
  • Constraint 2c: Email Functionality — Automated notifications and the ability to interact with the platform via email were required. Element was removed as email interaction is not possible.

Filter 3: Future Requirements

Four platforms remained after Filter 2: Discourse, Mattermost, Rocket.Chat, and Zulip. Three further constraints checked long-term viability:

  • Constraint 3a: Open Source Viability — All four have robust track records. Discourse has over one million communities; Mattermost has 30K+ GitHub stars.
  • Constraint 3b: Threat Model Alignment — All four can be deployed on-premise and have built-in moderation features for high-risk discussions.
  • Constraint 3c: Exit Strategy Feasibility — All four allow full data export without vendor lock-in and use standardised APIs for easy integration.

Assessment criteria

To choose between the four shortlisted platforms, criteria obtained from user stories, TBX research, and desk and user research were used. These criteria were organised into six categories:

Communication

Direct messaging, email integration, notifications, and search functionality.

User Management

Onboarding, permissions, profiles, and trust level systems.

Safety & Moderation

Content warnings, GDPR compliance, moderation tools, and privacy controls.

Recognition Systems

Engagement tracking, digital badges, impact reporting, and activism visibility.

Accessibility

Screen reader compatibility, multi-language support, and inclusive design.

Content Management

Bookmarking, pinning, templates, and easy sharing capabilities.

Detailed platform assessments

Discourse — Recommended

🟢 Strengths

  • Email Integration Excellence: Mailing list mode allows complete platform interaction via email, essential for engaging activists who prefer email communication.
  • Sophisticated Trust System: Five-tier progression system (New → Basic → Member → Regular → Leader) with automated advancement based on reading time and community engagement.
  • Advanced Moderation: Category-specific moderators with comprehensive tools including content warnings via spoiler tags and watched words for offensive language detection.
  • Recognition Systems: Built-in badge system with potential for Open Badges integration, supporting recognition of different activism types.
  • Content Management: Native support for pinning content, bookmarking functionality, and templates for consistent posting.

🟡 Limitations

  • Accessibility: Ongoing improvements but still challenges with screen reader compatibility and dropdown functionality. Discourse uses a non-standard dropdown navigation that is not 100% ARIA compliant.

Mattermost

🟢 Strengths

  • Security Leadership: Enterprise-grade privacy with comprehensive GDPR compliance and self-hosting capabilities.
  • Content Management: Robust bookmarking system (up to 50 bookmarks per channel) and message pinning functionality.
  • Safety & Moderation: Sophisticated channel moderation settings with granular permission controls.

🟡 Limitations

  • Core Communication: Limited to basic notifications, lacking digest functionality crucial for email-preferring demographics.
  • User Management: Role-based permissions without community progression pathways.
  • Accessibility: Basic accessibility features with limited screen reader optimisation.

🔴 Drawbacks

  • Recognition Systems: No native badge system or activism engagement tracking.

Rocket.Chat

🟢 Strengths

  • Accessibility: Significant improvements in version 6.8 with enhanced keyboard navigation and screen reader compatibility.
  • Security & Privacy: Strong encryption and self-hosting options with comprehensive user role management.
  • File Sharing: Excellent integration with Google Drive and other collaboration tools.

🟡 Limitations

  • Email Integration: Weak digest functionality, problematic for activists preferring email interaction.
  • User Management: Basic role-based system without community progression.
  • Content Management: Limited pinning functionality, particularly in direct messages.

🔴 Drawbacks

  • Recognition Systems: No engagement tracking or digital badge capabilities.

Zulip

🟢 Strengths

  • Communication: Topic-based threading and strong email digest capabilities.
  • Email Integration: Strong digest capabilities and incoming email gateway for email-first engagement.
  • Internationalisation: Robust multi-language support with translation capabilities.

🟡 Limitations

  • User Management: Recent flexible permissions system but lacks traditional community progression pathways.
  • Safety & Moderation: Minimal content hiding capabilities compared to other options (e.g. Discourse).
  • Safety & Moderation: Basic moderation tools without sophisticated category-specific controls.

🔴 Drawbacks

  • Recognition Systems: No badge system or activism visibility features.
