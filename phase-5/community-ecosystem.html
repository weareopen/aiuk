Building an AIUK Community Ecosystem

A holistic look at the digital tools, platform stacks, and activist workflows needed to support a vibrant AIUK community — beyond any single platform.

About this document

When thinking about the experience of the AIUK community, we need to consider the holistic experience of activists, educators, and staff members. While the new community platform will provide a focal point, the community itself is not constrained by a single platform.

By reviewing and analysing the digital tools used by other activist communities, we can begin to understand the additional spaces and functionality that the AIUK community may require. We can then make some recommendations as to what kind of 'platform stack' might support a vibrant community ecosystem.

Digital tools used by activist communities

Activist communities require robust, secure, and accessible platforms to facilitate meaningful discussion, decision-making, and collaboration. Unlike mainstream social media or chat apps, these platforms must prioritise privacy, decentralisation, and resilience against surveillance or censorship.

Platforms tend to serve one or two specific purposes and, chosen carefully, can work well in harmony with each other. The tools used for broadcast tend to meet people where they currently are — on centralised, algorithmically-controlled social networks controlled by Big Tech companies. An invite to a web page allows for greater control of the message, which then encourages visitors to learn more and get involved. From here, those who have made a commitment to the cause can join the community platform. For protests and actions that require coordination, encrypted chat apps are the best option.

The platform stack principle

This platform stack needs to be taken into account when choosing a community platform, as no one tool exists in isolation. It's part of a series of workflows that cover onboarding, engagement, and security.

📢

Broadcast

Meet people where they are — on mainstream social media and email lists. Drive awareness and initial engagement.

🌐

Web presence

A controlled web page to present the mission clearly and invite deeper involvement on your own terms.

🔒

Secure coordination

Encrypted chat apps for committed activists who need to coordinate actions with privacy and trust.

Key patterns and lessons

What activist networks have in common

  • Distributed responsibility — activist networks often distribute responsibility across local groups rather than relying on a single centralised platform.
  • Privacy measures — end-to-end encryption and metadata minimisation guard sensitive conversations against surveillance.
  • Separate channels — public outreach and internal coordination are typically kept on separate systems.
  • Low-bandwidth tools — text-based tools remain vital to accommodate participants in areas with limited connectivity.

Gently phasing out problematic platforms

AIUK should seek to distinguish how various corporate platforms are used, and at what point people are sent to the community platform. The organisation should work to help its activists and educators understand the politics around technology and its impact on human rights.

Making the switch from common platforms feels impossible, but there is a growing community of people who work to resist techno-political influence and who understand this influence on society. Over time, AIUK has an opportunity to support this important work as well as streamline its own online engagement work.

A continuous redirection approach

The key is a continuous, gentle redirection — helping activists and community members gradually become familiar with the community platform as an alternative space, rather than demanding an immediate switch away from tools they already use.

Appendix: CiviCRM case studies

Although the choice of CRM is out of scope for WAO's work with AIUK, in the course of research we found that CiviCRM is a mature, well-respected, open source, and extensible solution. It has bulk email and SMS capabilities, and so is useful alongside Signal for organising.

Amnesty International Vlaanderen

Focus: Human rights, fundraising, supporter engagement

Amnesty International Vlaanderen has used CiviCRM since 2011 to centralise donor, petition, and recruitment data. They developed a CiviCRM extension to automate the collection of data from street recruitment agencies, enabling automatic creation of contacts, recurring donations (SEPA mandates), and activities. This streamlined onboarding and reduced the time between donor signup and first payment.

Greenpeace Central and Eastern Europe (CEE)

Focus: Environmental activism, fundraising, data control

Greenpeace CEE migrated to CiviCRM in 2016, automating the processing of around 50,000 bank transactions annually, 89% of which are now handled automatically using the CiviBanking extension. This improved data control and allowed Greenpeace CEE to contribute code back to the CiviCRM project.
