Stakeholder Mapping
+ Glossary

A comprehensive mapping of everyone connected to the AIUK Community Platform project — categorised by interest and involvement — alongside a shared glossary of key terms used throughout the project.

About this document

This document was produced during Phase One as part of the project's planning and stakeholder engagement work. It builds on an earlier initial mapping exercise to provide a more detailed and refined picture of who is connected to the AIUK Community Platform project — and in what capacity.

Alongside the stakeholder map, the document includes a shared glossary of terms used across the project. This was important because the project brought together people from different parts of Amnesty — staff, volunteers, activists, and external partners — each with their own vocabulary for community organising, technology, and engagement.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Notes on the mapping

The mapping exercise surfaced several important observations that shaped how the project engaged its stakeholders throughout the subsequent phases.

Key observations from the mapping process

  • Overrepresentation of older activists — the initial mapping placed too many stakeholders in the "Manage Closely" quadrant, particularly older and more established activist groups. The revised mapping aimed for more proportionate representation.
  • Activist diversity — AIUK's activist community spans an enormous range: from Lead Activists and Country Coordinators to local groups, student networks, and specialist networks. This diversity had to be reflected accurately in any engagement strategy.
  • Volunteer vs staff distinctions — many key stakeholders are volunteers with no formal employment relationship with AIUK, requiring different approaches to communication and consent.
  • Sensitivity considerations — some activists operate in high-risk contexts (diaspora activists, those working on specific countries). Their privacy and security needs shaped how the project communicated with and about them.

Shared glossary of terms

The glossary was developed collaboratively by the project team to establish common language across AIUK staff, WAO consultants, and the wider activist community. Terms were drawn from the overlapping worlds of community organising, digital platforms, and human rights advocacy.

  • Activist

    An individual who takes action in support of Amnesty International UK's campaigns and human rights goals. Activists may be Lead Activists (with formal roles), network members, local group members, or individual supporters who take actions online or in person.

  • Lead Activist

    A volunteer who has taken on a defined leadership role within AIUK's activist community. There are approximately 360 Lead Activists, each with an Amnesty email address and access to safeguarding, GDPR, and diversity training.

  • Community Organiser

    A paid AIUK staff member (×2 at project start) responsible for supporting and mobilising the activist community. Community Organisers are primary users of the platform and central to its design and governance.

  • Country Coordinator

    A volunteer activist who specialises in human rights issues in a specific country or region. Country Coordinators connect the global Amnesty movement with UK-based activism on particular issues.

  • Network

    A thematic or identity-based community of activists within AIUK, such as the Feminist Network, Rainbow Network, Anti-Racism Network, or Children's Human Rights Network. Networks self-organise around shared causes or identities, and may have their own committees.

  • Local Group

    A geographically based group of Amnesty activists, typically in a town or city, who organise campaign actions and events locally. Local groups are one of the most established forms of Amnesty community in the UK.

  • Community Platform

    The online space that this project set out to design and build. Intended as infrastructure that enables activists to connect, share resources, and coordinate — not a replacement for existing channels, but a complement to them. The platform selected was Discourse, deployed at community.amnesty.org.uk.

  • Stakeholder

    Any individual or group with an interest in or connection to the AIUK Community Platform project. Stakeholders range from those who will actively use and moderate the platform (primary) to those who may be affected by it in a more peripheral way (tertiary).

  • Safeguarding

    Measures and protocols designed to protect vulnerable individuals — particularly children, young people, and those at risk — from harm. Safeguarding requirements directly shaped the platform's design decisions around permissions, age-gating, and moderation.

  • Moderation

    The process of reviewing and managing content and behaviour on the platform to ensure it aligns with community guidelines and the code of conduct. The project agreed that moderation should not be the sole responsibility of volunteers — a hybrid approach was favoured.

  • Right Holder

    In Amnesty's framing, the individuals whose rights are at stake — those directly affected by human rights abuses. Right holders are distinct from activists who campaign on their behalf, and their needs are central to how the platform was designed.

  • WAO (We Are Open Co-op)

    The cooperative that designed and delivered the AIUK Community Platform project alongside AIUK. WAO provided strategic facilitation, community design, research, and technical expertise across all project phases.

  • AIUK

    Amnesty International UK — the UK section of Amnesty International, the client organisation for this project. AIUK's Activism and Education Directorate led the project internally.

  • Architecture of Participation (AoP)

    A framework used to design the community structure, defining how different roles, permissions, and participation pathways are organised to encourage meaningful engagement. Applied during the platform configuration phase.

  • Pilot

    A structured trial phase in which a defined group of users tests the platform before it is opened more widely. The AIUK pilot ran across four phases from February to April 2026, starting with community leaders and moderators and expanding progressively.

  • Discourse

    The community forum platform selected for the AIUK Community Platform, hosted by Communiteq. The official URL is community.amnesty.org.uk. Selected following a structured user research and platform testing process in Phases One and Two.
