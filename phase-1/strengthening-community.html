Strengthening Community:
Building Bridges for Human Rights with AIUK

An early strategic framing of what the AIUK community platform set out to do — and why building bridges between activists matters for human rights work.

About this document

This document was produced at the start of Phase One, alongside the Project Charter, as part of establishing the strategic framing for the AIUK Community Platform project. It sets out the rationale for building a dedicated online space for Amnesty International UK's activist community — grounding the project in the practical realities of how human rights organising works.

The document draws on the broader AIUK strategy and the Activism and Education Directorate's goals to articulate why the community platform is worth building.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Original on Google Drive (unavailable after May 2026)

Context and rationale

Amnesty International UK works with a wide and varied community of activists — from Lead Activists and Country Coordinators to local groups, student networks, and specialist networks like the Feminist Network and Rainbow Network. These people are spread across the UK and often work in relative isolation from one another.

The challenge the platform project was set up to address: how do you help people who care about the same things find each other, share knowledge, and act more effectively together?

🤝

Connection

Activists often don't know who else is working on similar campaigns. The platform creates a shared space to find and connect with peers.

📚

Knowledge sharing

Practical know-how — how to run a local group, navigate a campaign, or support affected communities — gets lost when it lives only in people's heads.

⚡

Collective action

Movements become more effective when people can coordinate across geography and campaign type, not just in isolated pockets.

What "strengthening community" means

The title of this document reflects a specific choice of language. The project team chose "strengthening" rather than "building" community because AIUK's activist community already exists — it has depth, history, and real relationships. The platform is not about creating something from scratch; it's about removing barriers and creating better conditions for a community that is already doing important work.

The platform as infrastructure, not a destination

A recurring theme in the early framing: the platform should feel like infrastructure — useful, reliable, and present when you need it — rather than a place you have to go to. It works best when it fits into the natural rhythms of activist life, not when it demands attention for its own sake.

The document also addresses the specific challenge of bridge-building — connecting people across the different networks, regions, and campaign areas that make up AIUK's activist community. Many activists are deeply embedded in one area (a local group, a specific campaign, a specialist network) but have little visibility of what's happening elsewhere.

Human rights as the connective tissue

The document grounds the platform project in Amnesty's mission and the specific context of UK-based activism. Human rights work in the UK spans an enormous range of issues — from climate justice and refugee rights to anti-death penalty campaigning, disability rights, and education. The platform needs to hold all of this diversity while still feeling coherent.

A shared identity across diverse work

What unites AIUK's activist community isn't that everyone works on the same issues — it's a shared commitment to human rights and to Amnesty's way of working. The platform's design needed to reflect this: structured enough to be useful, open enough to welcome the full breadth of what activists do.

Implications for the project

This early framing document had a direct influence on several decisions made later in the project:

How this shaped the project

  • Community consultation was central — the research phase (Phase Two) was designed around listening to activists before making any technology decisions
  • Breadth of stakeholders — the stakeholder mapping captured the full diversity of AIUK's activist ecosystem, not just the most visible groups
  • Co-creation workshops — the strategy design phase (Phase Four) involved activists in shaping what the community would look like
  • Technology followed strategy — the decision not to select a platform until after the community strategy was complete (Phase Five) stemmed from this framing
