Online Safety Act
Advice and Guidance

A practical compliance guide for AIUK's Discourse community platform — covering age assurance, risk assessment, content moderation, data protection, and transparency obligations under the Online Safety Act 2023.

About this document

The Online Safety Act 2023 (OSA) represents the UK's most significant internet regulation to date. It places legal duties on platforms to protect users from illegal content and shield children from harmful material.

Amnesty International UK (AIUK) plans to host a Discourse community platform for activists and educators. Despite being hosted in the EU, the OSA applies because the platform will have UK users and be accessible to UK residents.

The decision to include children rather than restricting access to 18+ carries specific compliance obligations, particularly around age assurance, risk assessment, and content moderation.

Important note: This guidance is prepared by We Are Open Co-op (WAO) and provides a step-by-step approach to OSA compliance whilst balancing AIUK's mission to engage young activists with statutory child protection requirements. WAO are not lawyers, and AIUK's Data Protection Officer (DPO) should provide specific advice, especially around how political opinions are processed (Article 9 UK GDPR).

1. Understanding the Regulatory Framework

1.1. OSA Scope and Enforcement

The OSA has "extraterritorial application," meaning EU-hosted platforms like AIUK's community platform must comply if they have UK users or target UK audiences. Ofcom serves as the independent regulator with enforcement powers including fines up to £18 million or 10% of global revenue, whichever is greater. The child safety provisions came fully into force on 25 July 2025.

OSA Rollout Phases

The OSA rolled out in three phases, at least two of which will apply to the AIUK community platform:

  • Illegal harms duties (March 2025): Platforms must protect users from illegal content including child sexual abuse material, terrorism content, and material inciting violence
  • Child safety duties (July 2025): Platforms must prevent children accessing harmful or age-inappropriate content
  • Categorised service duties (ongoing): Additional requirements for larger platforms based on reach and functionality

1.2. Definitions of Children Under the OSA

The OSA defines children as anyone under 18. By allowing teenage users, AIUK's platform will be providing services to children under the legal definition, triggering specific child safety duties.

2. Conduct a Children's Access Assessment

2.1. Legal Requirement

The OSA requires every in-scope platform to complete a Children's Access Assessment (CAA) to determine whether children are likely to access the service. AIUK has already identified that children will be permitted users, meaning the platform definitively meets the "child user condition".

2.2. Two-Stage Assessment Process

Ofcom guidance establishes a two-stage process:

2.2.1. Age Assurance Assessment

Platforms must evaluate whether highly effective age assurance mechanisms are in place that prevent children from accessing the service. If "highly effective" age assurance exists and works reliably, platforms may conclude children cannot access the service and avoid further child safety duties.

For AIUK, this stage is moot because the platform intentionally permits children. The platform will not have highly effective age assurance preventing all under-18s.

2.2.2. Child User Condition

Platforms must determine if either:

  • A significant number of children are using the service, or
  • The service is of a kind likely to attract a significant number of children

"Significant number" depends on context. Even relatively small numbers of children could constitute "significant" if they represent a material proportion of the user base. AIUK's deliberate inclusion of children means this condition is met.

2.3. Documentation Requirements

CAA Documentation Obligations

AIUK must document the CAA outcome and supporting evidence. This assessment must be repeated annually or whenever significant changes occur to the platform's design, operation, or evidence emerges about age assurance effectiveness.

3. Implement Age Assurance

3.1. Understanding Age Assurance vs Age Verification

Age assurance encompasses any method of estimating or verifying user age. Age verification refers specifically to hard-identifier methods (passports, driving licences, credit cards) that provide high confidence in age.

3.2. Age Assurance Technology Options

Government research identifies four broad categories of age assurance methods, each with distinct inclusion and privacy considerations.

3.2.1. Hard Identifiers (Age Verification)

Users verify age using government-issued ID, credit cards, or other documents linked to minimum age thresholds. This approach provides high confidence but presents significant exclusion risks.

Advantages

  • High accuracy and confidence in age determination
  • Well-established in regulated sectors like online gambling

Disadvantages

  • Many children lack suitable ID documents
  • Creates acute barriers for vulnerable groups including care-experienced young people
  • Users express security concerns about submitting ID information
  • Not privacy-preserving

3.2.2. Verified Parental Consent (VPC)

An adult verifies their age and grants permission for a child user, typically using credit card verification.

Advantages

  • Natural fit for some situations where parents engage with children's online activity
  • Already widely used in paid services

Disadvantages

  • Inappropriate for teenagers seeking privacy from parents
  • Problematic for care-experienced youth lacking consistent parental figures
  • Not suitable for activist communities where independence from family is important

3.2.3. Behavioural Data and Artificial Intelligence

AI analyses user behaviour patterns (content interactions, typing patterns, browsing habits) to estimate age.

Advantages

  • Privacy-preserving and relatively frictionless
  • Most inclusive for vulnerable groups
  • No ID documents required

Disadvantages

  • Provides estimation rather than verification
  • Less accurate for younger users with limited platform history
  • May misclassify atypical users
  • Requires substantial user data collection

3.2.4. Biometric Data and Artificial Intelligence

AI estimates age from biometric data, typically facial scanning.

Advantages

  • No ID documents required
  • Can be privacy-preserving when properly implemented
  • Relatively frictionless user experience

Disadvantages

  • Performs less accurately for darker skin tones and atypical facial structures
  • Raises privacy concerns from some users
  • May be inaccessible to users with disabilities affecting facial features

3.3. Recommended Approach for AIUK

Given the specific context of a community being inclusive of teenage activists, a layered approach combining multiple methods offers the best balance of accuracy, inclusion, and privacy protection. Research shows platforms are increasingly adopting this strategy to improve accuracy whilst maximising inclusion.

Recommended Layered Age Assurance Implementation

  • Self-declaration as first gateway — Users affirm they are 13+ during registration. Use neutral age declaration screens that don't display the minimum age requirement prominently, reducing incentive to lie. Prevent users from immediately re-entering different birthdates if initially declined.
  • Email-based age estimation — Cross-reference email addresses with educational institution databases or age-associated email patterns. Ofcom guidance recognises email-based estimation as potentially highly effective when properly implemented.
  • Behavioural monitoring — Implement ongoing age estimation based on user interaction patterns. This catches users who bypassed initial checks. Discourse's existing user trust levels and engagement metrics can support this approach.
  • Risk-based verification — Reserve 'hard' identifier verification for edge cases where behavioural signals consistently suggest under-age users or where access to particularly sensitive areas requires higher assurance.

4. Complete Comprehensive Risk Assessment

4.1. Legal Obligation

Platforms likely to be accessed by children must conduct risk assessments identifying potential harms and implementing proportionate safety measures. Existing platforms had until 24 July 2025 to complete initial children's risk assessments, so AIUK should complete this as soon as possible given the intention for children aged 13+ to access the platform.

4.2. Risk Assessment Framework

Ofcom provides a four-step process, contextualised below.

4.2.1. Identify functionalities that could present risks

For a Discourse platform, consider:

  • User-to-user messaging (direct messages)
  • Public posting and replies
  • Profile information and customisation
  • File uploads and image sharing
  • External link sharing
  • Search functionality
  • User mentions and notifications

4.2.2. Assess likelihood and severity of harm

Ofcom identifies content harmful to children including:

  • Bullying and harassment
  • Content promoting self-harm or suicide
  • Eating disorder content
  • Serious violence
  • Hateful content
  • Content promoting dangerous activities

Additional Risk Considerations for an Activist Community

  • Exposure to graphic documentation of human rights abuses
  • Coordinated harassment from opposing groups
  • Surveillance and targeting by hostile state actors
  • Doxxing and privacy violations

4.2.3. Determine if risks are significant

Consider whether harms could occur with sufficient likelihood and severity to require mitigation. Given AIUK's focus on human rights activism, the risk profile differs from general social platforms.

4.2.4. Implement proportionate mitigations

Safety measures must be proportionate to identified risks.

4.2.5. Documentation Requirements

Document the risk assessment process, findings, and mitigation decisions. Update assessments when platform changes occur or new evidence about risks emerges.

5. Implement Content Moderation and Safety Measures

5.1. Core Content Moderation Obligations

OSA Content Moderation Requirements

The OSA requires platforms to:

  • Prevent illegal content from appearing
  • Remove illegal content swiftly when identified
  • Prevent children accessing harmful or age-inappropriate content
  • Implement proactive detection technologies
  • Use content moderation tools and human review where appropriate

5.2. Discourse-Specific Safety Features

Discourse provides multiple safety tools that can support OSA compliance, including the following.

5.2.1. Content Controls

  • Moderation queues for first posts by new users
  • Trust level system that restricts new user capabilities
  • Flagging system for user reports
  • Automatic spam and abuse detection
  • Content filters and word lists
  • Rate limiting to prevent flooding

5.2.2. User Safety Features

  • User blocking and muting
  • Private/public category controls
  • Silencing and suspending user accounts
  • IP address blocking
  • Required approval for new accounts

5.2.3. Visibility Controls

  • Category-level permissions
  • Topic visibility restrictions
  • Age-gated content areas
  • Content warnings and tags

5.3. Recommended Safety Architecture

To comply with the OSA and include children aged 13+ WAO recommend:

🔒

Graduated Access

New users enter at restricted trust levels, gaining privileges as they demonstrate appropriate behaviour. This aligns with Discourse's existing trust level system.

🗂️

Content Categorisation

Separate potentially distressing human rights documentation from general discussion areas. Require age verification or additional warnings before accessing graphic content categories.

🔍

Proactive Monitoring

Use Discourse's flagging system alongside automated filters for priority harmful content including self-harm, suicide, and child sexual abuse material.

👥

Human Moderation

Maintain trained moderators familiar with both human rights context and child safety concerns. Research shows human moderation remains important alongside automated tools.

🚨

Clear Reporting

Implement accessible reporting tools and communicate response timeframes. The OSA requires swift action on illegal content and harmful content flagged by users.

5.4. Terms of Service Requirements

The OSA mandates clear, accessible Terms of Service that children can understand. AIUK must ensure the following relating to the community platform:

  • State what content is prohibited
  • Explain enforcement procedures
  • Describe age restrictions and verification processes
  • Outline user rights including appeals
  • Write in language accessible to children
  • Apply these terms consistently — inconsistent enforcement can trigger Ofcom complaints and enforcement action

6. Establish Complaints and Redress Mechanisms

6.1. Legal Requirements

Platforms must provide clear, accessible mechanisms for users to report problems and challenge moderation decisions. For children, these systems must be age-appropriate and easy to understand.

6.2. Implementation Recommendations

Complaints Mechanism Checklist

  • Multiple reporting channels — Offer in-platform flagging, direct contact with moderators, and escalation to platform administrators
  • Clear communication — Explain what happens after a report is submitted, expected response times, and how decisions are made
  • Appeals process — Allow users to appeal moderation decisions with clear timelines for responses
  • External escalation — Inform users they can raise super-complaints with expert organisations or ultimately escalate to Ofcom
  • Documentation — Maintain records of complaints, responses, and resolution outcomes for transparency reporting

7. Ensure Data Protection Compliance

7.1. Intersection of OSA and UK GDPR

The OSA operates alongside data protection obligations. The UK GDPR sets 13 as the age of digital consent, but the OSA's child safety provisions extend to all under-18s.

7.2. Special Category Data

AIUK's community platform will process or infer political opinions through posts, profiles, tags, and moderation, which are special category data. This will have to be specifically referenced in the Data Protection Impact Assessment (DPIA).

The 'Manifestly Made Public' Clause

The 'manifestly made public' clause around political opinions only applies when a user has intentionally made the information public to a 'genuinely public audience'. Closed or limited forums do not typically satisfy this threshold, so the clause is unlikely to apply to AIUK's community platform. AIUK's DPO should be consulted on this point.

7.3. Data Minimisation Principles

Age assurance creates tension between collecting data to verify age and data minimisation principles. The Information Commissioner's Office (ICO) has clarified that the Age Appropriate Design Code (2022) doesn't prevent collecting data for age assurance purposes.

Data Minimisation Best Practices

  • Purpose limitation — Collect age data only for age assurance and safety purposes
  • Storage limitation — Retain age verification data only as long as necessary
  • Security measures — Implement robust data security for sensitive age verification information
  • Transparency — Clearly explain what age data is collected, how it's used, and how it's protected
  • Third-party processors — When using external age verification services, ensure they meet GDPR standards

7.4. Special Protections for Children's Data

The UK Age Appropriate Design Code requires:

  • High privacy settings by default for child users
  • Data collection minimised to what's necessary for service provision
  • Location tracking disabled unless strictly necessary
  • No use of children's data for profiling or targeted advertising
  • Transparent privacy information written for children

8. Develop Transparency and Reporting Practices

8.1. Transparency Obligations

Whilst AIUK's platform likely won't qualify as a 'Category 1' service under the OSA (which would require full transparency reports), good practice nevertheless includes:

  • Public safety statement — Explain how the platform approaches child safety, what measures are in place, and how risks are managed
  • Terms of Service — Make safety policies visible and accessible
  • User communications — Regularly communicate safety information to users, particularly when policies or features change
  • Age assurance disclosure — Explain what age verification methods are used and why

8.2. Internal Documentation

Maintain comprehensive internal records including:

  • Children's access assessments (updated annually)
  • Risk assessments (updated when significant changes occur)
  • Moderation decisions and rationales
  • Complaints and their resolutions
  • Safety incidents and responses
  • Age assurance effectiveness reviews

8.3. Ofcom Contact

Designate a direct contact point for Ofcom correspondence regarding compliance queries or enforcement actions. Respond promptly to Ofcom requests, as delays can escalate enforcement responses.

9. Case Studies and Examples

9.1. Discourse Platform Approaches

Discourse's own guidance acknowledges the OSA challenges. The company emphasises that compliance depends not only on software capabilities but on how administrators configure and operate their communities. Discourse provides tools but places responsibility on community operators to implement age assurance and safety measures appropriate to their context.

One Discourse community operator (Weather-Watch, 2025) documented their OSA compliance approach on the platform's forum. They implemented:

  • Age verification at registration using email verification against known databases
  • Layered moderation with automated filters and human review
  • Content categorisation separating general discussion from potentially harmful material
  • Regular risk assessment reviews tied to feature updates

9.2. Strengthened Parental Consent Case Study

High Drop-off From Strict Verification

One platform interviewed for government research (UK Government, 2023) strengthened its Verified Parental Consent process by requiring credit card or ID verification. This resulted in 50–80% user drop-off. The platform was not able to determine whether drop-off resulted from the effort required, lack of ID access, or privacy concerns.

This case study reinforces why layered approaches starting with lighter-touch methods are preferable for teenagers who merit more autonomy than younger children.

9.3. Learning from France

France's data protection authority CNIL and media regulator ARCOM developed detailed age verification guidance emphasising privacy-preserving methods (Baker McKenzie, 2025). Their approach advocates:

  • Double-blind architecture where verification and access-granting are separated
  • Favouring cryptographic proofs of age over identity disclosure
  • Independent third-party certification of age assurance systems
  • Proportionality based on content risk

This privacy-first approach offers a model for activist organisations like AIUK where user anonymity and security are priorities.

10. Risk Analysis

10.1. Compliance Risks

10.1.1. Inadequate Age Assurance

If AIUK relies solely on self-declaration without additional measures, Ofcom could deem age assurance ineffective. This exposes the platform to enforcement action.

Mitigation: Implement layered age assurance combining self-declaration with email verification, behavioural monitoring, and reserved capacity for hard identifier verification when needed.

10.1.2. Insufficient Risk Assessment

Failure to identify and address risks of harm to teenagers could breach child safety duties.

Mitigation: Conduct thorough initial risk assessment and update regularly, particularly when adding new features or if incidents occur.

10.1.3. Slow Content Removal

The OSA requires swift removal of illegal content. Delays could trigger enforcement action, particularly for priority illegal content like child sexual abuse material or terrorist content.

Mitigation: Establish clear moderation workflows with defined response timeframes. Use automated detection for priority illegal content categories.

10.1.4. Inadequate Complaints Handling

Failing to provide accessible, age-appropriate reporting mechanisms or not responding to complaints appropriately breaches OSA requirements.

Mitigation: Implement multiple reporting channels, clear communication about processes, and documented appeals procedures.

10.2. Operational Risks

10.2.1. Moderator Capacity

Human moderation requires trained staff understanding both human rights context and child safety. Insufficient moderation capacity could allow harmful content to persist.

Mitigation: Invest in training moderators on OSA requirements, child safety concerns, and trauma-informed practices for reviewing human rights documentation.

10.2.2. Technology Limitations

Automated content filters may generate false positives (blocking legitimate human rights content) or false negatives (missing harmful content).

Mitigation: Combine automated tools with human review. Tune filters to your community's specific context. Allow appeals of automated decisions.

10.2.3. User Circumvention

Young people may attempt to bypass age assurance. Research indicates vulnerable youth might be highly motivated to gain access because online communities provide important inclusion and connection.

Mitigation: Ongoing behavioural monitoring can identify users who bypassed initial checks. Education about why age limits exist may increase compliance.

10.3. Mission Risks

10.3.1. Deterring Legitimate Young Activists

Overly restrictive age assurance could exclude genuine teenage activists AIUK wants to engage.

Mitigation: Use inclusive age assurance methods that don't require ID documents many young people lack. Layered approaches maximise inclusion.

10.3.2. Chilling Effect on Sensitive Discussions

Excessive content moderation or surveillance could deter open discussion of human rights issues.

Mitigation: Use privacy-preserving age assurance. Communicate clearly about what content moderation occurs and why. Focus moderation on genuine harms rather than legitimate human rights documentation.

10.3.3. Activist Security Concerns

Age verification requiring ID submission creates security risks for activists in hostile environments.

Mitigation: Use privacy-preserving age assurance that doesn't require ID submission to third parties. If hard identifier verification is needed in specific cases, use certified services with strong data protection.

10.4. Reputational Risks

10.4.1. Ofcom Enforcement Action

Public enforcement action including fines or service restriction orders would damage AIUK's reputation.

Mitigation: Prioritise compliance, document your approach thoroughly, and engage proactively with Ofcom if questions arise.

10.4.2. Child Safety Incidents

High-profile cases of children being harmed through the platform would generate significant criticism and negative publicity.

Mitigation: Robust risk assessment, proactive safety measures, and swift incident response.

10.4.3. Excluding Young Activists

Being seen to exclude young people AIUK claims to support could contradict organisational mission.

Mitigation: Choose inclusive age assurance methods. Communicate clearly about the legal requirements driving age assurance decisions.

11. Technology Recommendations

11.1. Discourse Configuration

Core Safety Settings:

Recommended Discourse Safety Configuration

  • Registration controls — Require email verification, implement registration screening questions, enable manual approval for new accounts if needed
  • Trust level progression — Configure Discourse's trust levels to limit new user capabilities until they demonstrate appropriate behaviour
  • Content filters — Implement watched words lists for terms associated with self-harm, suicide, eating disorders, and other priority harms
  • Flagging thresholds — Set appropriate thresholds for automated actions when users flag content
  • Rate limiting — Configure post limits, reply limits, and message limits to prevent harassment and flooding
  • Category permissions — Create graduated access with some categories restricted to higher trust levels or requiring additional verification

Yoti is worth exploring as it provides multiple methods for users to prove their age online. Although they offer over 10 different ways to do this, the options most relevant to AIUK community members are:

Zero-knowledge proofs (ZKP) rely on adding government ID to a wallet provided by a third-party provider. Whilst this might be a good option in future, it is likely to be confusing at the time of AIUK's community platform launch.

12. Implementation Timeline

12.1. Immediate Actions

Immediate Actions Required

  • Complete formal Children's Access Assessment documenting that children will access the platform
  • Conduct comprehensive risk assessment identifying harms children might encounter
  • Designate Ofcom contact point
  • Review and strengthen Terms of Service to meet OSA requirements

12.2. Short-Term Actions

Short-Term Actions

  • Implement layered age assurance combining self-declaration, email verification, and behavioural monitoring
  • Configure Discourse safety features including trust levels, content filters, and moderation queues
  • Establish content moderation workflows and train moderators on OSA requirements
  • Create user-facing safety information and reporting guides
  • Develop internal documentation templates for tracking compliance activities

12.3. Ongoing Actions

Ongoing Compliance Activities

  • Monitor age assurance effectiveness monthly and adjust as needed
  • Review and update risk assessments quarterly or after significant changes
  • Conduct annual Children's Access Assessment
  • Analyse moderation metrics and user reports to identify emerging issues
  • Update policies and procedures as Ofcom guidance evolves

13. Conclusion

The OSA represents a significant shift in UK internet regulation, placing substantial obligations on platforms serving children aged under 18. For AIUK's Discourse community including teenage activists, compliance requires implementing age assurance, conducting thorough risk assessments, establishing robust content moderation, and maintaining comprehensive documentation.

The recommended approach balances statutory obligations with AIUK's mission to engage young activists. Layered age assurance maximises inclusion whilst meeting effectiveness standards. Privacy-preserving methods protect activist security. Risk-proportionate safety measures address genuine harms without excessive censorship.

Starting with teenagers rather than 18+ increases compliance complexity but remains achievable with proper planning and resource allocation. The alternative — initially restricting the platform to 18+ and later expanding to teenage activists — would require conducting new risk assessments and implementing age assurance at that later stage anyway.

By following this guidance, AIUK can create a community platform that complies with the OSA whilst fulfilling its mission to engage and support young human rights activists.
