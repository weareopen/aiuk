Community Management Plan

The operational handbook for running the AIUK community platform — covering moderation, governance, growth thresholds, management rhythms, and continuous improvement.

Executive Summary

As documented in our Processes and Workflows document, a community is more than its platform.

Our Vision

This plan outlines the strategy to transition AIUK from isolated silos of activity into a connected, resilient network. Our goal is a community where members learn from and support each other, moving through journeys that benefit from our collective expertise and resources. We are moving from a model of lopsided, staff-dependent engagement to one of sustainable, peer-led activism.

Why this matters

A thriving community is a living network of relationships. By distributing leadership and supporting peer-to-peer connections, we increase our capacity to act for human rights without proportionally increasing the burden on staff. This shift ensures that every activist — whether a long-standing member or a new arrival — feels valued, equipped, and connected.

How we will get there

This document serves as our operational handbook. It details:

  • Scalable Moderation: How we share the workload between staff and other community members
  • Governance: The clear rules and roles that keep the community safe and focused
  • Growth: The triggers and strategies that will allow us to expand sustainably
  • Continuous Improvement: How we will use data and feedback to keep getting better

A living document

This document will be reviewed quarterly to ensure it evolves alongside our community's needs.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

1. Scalable Moderation Systems

1.1 Suggested Moderation Approach

1.1.1 Team Structure

In the post-pilot 'Establishment Stage' (3–12 months) we recommend that AIUK uses the following approach when approaching moderation:

Recommended staffing (Establishment Stage)

  • AIUK staff moderators: 3 (Community Manager + 2 others)
  • Volunteer moderators: 3 (target of 5 after 12 months)
  • Average individual time per week dedicated to moderation: 3–4 hours
  • Cross-team coordination: Activism & Education (primary), DDaT (technical support), Campaigns & Comms (content elevation)

1.1.2 How we moderate together

🤝

Rules with the community

Our staff and volunteer moderators work as a team to create the guidelines that keep our spaces healthy. We avoid imposing top-down rules — the people who spend the most time in the community know best what it needs.

🌱

Every generation of activist

Our community brings together decades of experience and fresh energy. Long-standing members (many aged 55+) with deep knowledge work alongside newer activists. Our moderation style helps experienced members mentor others while ensuring newcomers feel welcome.

📋

Transparent decision-making

We have clear, written agreements on when a moderator can act alone and when they need to ask for help. Everyone, from community members to staff, knows exactly how decisions are made and who is responsible for what.

Clear roles for staff and community leaders:

  • Community leader moderators handle day-to-day conversations and help the community govern itself
  • Staff moderators ensure overall safety, step in when specific authority is needed, and support community leaders with guidance

1.1.3 Moderation Workflows

The Community Manager should provide future Admins, site-wide moderators and category moderators with step-by-step moderation procedures, response templates and internal procedures for managing tensions should they arise on the platform.

Step-by-step moderation procedures as well as escalation procedures have already been created to support this.

1.1.4 Handling Issues: Escalation Matrix

We categorise issues into three levels to ensure the right person handles them.

Level Handled by Examples Action Escalation
Level 1 — Low Category Moderators Spam, minor disagreements, off-topic posts Flag post, send standard reminder message, hide post if necessary None needed unless the user persists
Level 2 — Medium Site-wide Moderators Persistent disruption, Code of Conduct breaches, aggressive behaviour Direct warning, temporary silence (suspension), review of user history Document in the moderator log
Level 3 — High Admins / Legal Hate speech, safeguarding concerns, legal threats, serious harassment Immediate suspension, hide all content, escalate to Legal/Safeguarding team Immediate report to Leadership

1.2 Growing Without Breaking

To grow the community without overloading our staff, we use three connected strategies:

  • Automation handles repetitive tasks
  • Community leaders share the responsibility of moderation
  • Self-governance helps members solve problems together

A step-by-step cycle

We do not do everything at once. First, we built the technical tools and automation. Next, we are using those tools to support and train moderators. Finally, as moderators gain confidence, the community takes more control. This moves us from a staff-led group to a mature, member-led community.

Pacing ourselves

We space this work out over the first year. Rushing would overwhelm the team and confuse our members. By taking our time, we can test and refine each part before moving to the next.

Strategy Implementation Timeline Responsible Team
Discourse Trust Levels Configure automatic permissions progression based on engagement Month 1 DDaT
Automated Moderation Implement spam filters, flagging thresholds, watch/mute lists Months 1–3 DDaT with Activism team input
Community Self-Moderation Enable flag system, peer review, user muting capabilities Months 1–6 DDaT implementation
Moderator Recruitment Identify experienced activists, co-design role description Months 3–6 Activism & Education
Training Programme Moderation and leadership training for community organisers Months 3–6 Activism & Education
Mentorship Programme Long-term activists mentor newer members, document structures Months 6–12 Activism & Education
Content Elevation Process Staff "pull up" interesting themes to landing page Ongoing Activism team with Comms

1.3 The Moderator Lifecycle

We treat our moderators as a vital part of the team. This is how they join, grow, and eventually step down.

1.3.1 Recruitment

We need moderators who know the platform and share our values. Candidates should have:

  • Experience: Active participation in AIUK activism for at least 6 months
  • Engagement: Trust Level 3+ on the platform (or similar involvement)
  • Values: A clear understanding of human rights and our community guidelines
  • Safety: A willingness to complete safeguarding training
  • Approach: A commitment to working with the community, not just enforcing rules
  • Diversity: We welcome people from all regions, ages, and backgrounds

1.3.2 Training and Support

New site-wide moderators receive structured preparation and ongoing support to build their confidence and capability. The initial training format and duration will be co-designed with the first cohort, recognising that those doing the work are best placed to identify what preparation they need.

Support mechanisms include:

  • Moderation guides with clear procedures for when to moderate, how to report concerns, and escalation paths
  • Regular check-ins to discuss challenges and share learning
  • A moderator-only category on the platform for peer support and coordination
  • Monthly coordination calls bringing together community leaders and staff moderators
  • Decision-making frameworks documenting when to act independently versus when to escalate to staff
  • Mandatory safeguarding training for all moderators
  • Digital security training focused on protecting both themselves and vulnerable community members

1.3.3 Moderator Recognition

We value the hard work site-wide moderators do to keep our community healthy. We show this through:

  • Visibility: Clear badges and status on the platform
  • Public thanks: Mentions in community updates and reports
  • Progression: Opportunities to step up into senior roles
  • Development: Access to exclusive training
  • Influence: A real say in future decisions

1.3.4 Offboarding

When a moderator wants to step down, we celebrate their contribution. They move to "Alumni" status, keeping their badge but losing moderation tools. This allows them to retire gracefully without feeling guilty.

2. Governance and Decision-Making

2.1 Governance Structure

We have clear rules about who decides what to avoid confusion and delays.

  • The Community Manager makes the day-to-day operational choices
  • Leadership handles major strategic or legal issues
  • Community members have a structured way to shape the rules that affect them

2.1.1 The Community Manager's role

The Community Manager makes the operational decisions that keep the community running smoothly. They handle daily platform choices, plan content and engagement, and coordinate work across teams. They are also responsible for recruiting and supporting moderators, as well as clarifying routine policies.

2.1.2 When leadership steps in

Senior leaders handle the high-stakes decisions involving strategy, law, or money. This includes:

  • Significant rule changes: Major updates to policies or privacy practices
  • Financial approvals: Spending above the agreed budget
  • Safety and legality: Serious legal issues or safeguarding concerns
  • Reputation: Issues that conflict with the organisation's public stance

2.1.3 How members have their say

We ask members to help make the decisions that affect them. This builds trust and ensures our rules work in practice.

  • Regular feedback: Quarterly reviews of how the platform is working
  • Co-design: Working with members to design how trust is built
  • Moderator input: Site-wide moderators help shape our policies
  • Surveys: Opportunities to share views at every stage
  • Representation: We listen to everyone, from new recruits to long-term activists in every region

2.2 Working Across Teams

Three teams manage the platform together. Each has clear authority over its own area but consults the others to ensure everything works smoothly.

Team Leads on Consults
Activism & Education Team Community engagement, moderation rules, and mentorship Technical teams on what is possible; Comms on how to share success
DDaT Team (Digital/Technical) Building the platform, security, and hosting Activism teams on user needs; Legal on privacy rules
Campaigns & Comms Team Promoting the community externally and celebrating milestones Activism teams to check community feeling before posting

2.3 Resolving Conflict

Conflict happens in every active community. We have clear, fair procedures to handle disputes and keep trust high.

2.3.1 Between Members

When individuals disagree, our community guidelines set the standard. Members can flag concerning behaviour, and moderators will step in using agreed rules. Complex issues are escalated to staff, and while serious breaches may lead to suspension, there is always a clear right to appeal.

2.3.2 Between Groups

Sometimes regional or specialist groups clash. We resolve this by focusing on our shared human rights mission, often using experienced activists or staff to mediate. We document what is agreed and check back later to ensure the solution holds. If needed, the Community Manager makes the final decision.

2.3.3 Appealing Decisions

Members can question moderation decisions without fear. We explain why an action was taken and offer a clear way to appeal through staff review. The Community Manager has the final say, and we share general patterns of issues to be transparent without naming individuals.

2.4 Keeping Our Rules Up to Date

We review our policies regularly to make sure they still work for the community.

3. Understanding Our Limits

3.1 Current Capacity

Before we grow, we need to know what we can handle right now. At launch, and then every three months, we will measure how many members, posts, and leaders our current team can support effectively without burnout. This gives us a baseline for sustainable growth.

3.2 When to Scale

We set clear "triggers" to tell us when it is time to expand. Instead of waiting for problems to happen, we agree on specific member counts or activity levels that signal it is time to act. By watching these numbers, we can add resources or new systems before we get overwhelmed.

Metric / Threshold Action Required Resource Implications Timeline
500 members Recruit 10 more moderators Training time investment (20–30 hours) Month 6
1,000 members Implement additional automation, assess staff moderator capacity Plugin costs (£X), potential 0.2 FTE staff Month 9
50 topics/week Recruit specialist moderators for high-traffic categories Training investment Month 6–9
25+ local groups Create regional coordinator roles Coordination structure, documentation Month 9
10+ flags/day Review moderation procedures, increase moderator hours Moderator capacity assessment As needed
Sense of community score >24 Transition to maturity stage approaches, enable more self-governance Policy updates, trust level adjustments Month 12+
Sub-communities forming Support "mitosis" with dedicated spaces and autonomous governance Platform structure changes Month 18+

3.3 Planning For the Future

3.3.1 If we reach 4,000 members

We can manage this by upgrading our existing systems. We would need to ensure Communiteq has capacity, more volunteer moderators, and smarter automation for tasks like spam detection.

3.3.2 If we reach 10,000 members

At this scale, we need structural change. We would require a larger dedicated team, a tiered system of senior moderators, and possibly AI tools to help manage the load. Sub-communities would likely need to govern themselves more independently.

3.3.3 Limits to watch

To grow safely, we must monitor key risks:

  • Staff capacity: We cannot grow without enough trained leaders
  • Costs: Fees for verification or hosting could rise
  • Culture: Rapid growth might dilute our values
  • Recruitment: We need a steady stream of new moderators
  • Safety: More members means more complex conflicts to manage

Watching these factors helps us fix problems before they stop our progress.

We have a set schedule to keep the community healthy without letting tasks pile up.

Day-to-day care

  • Review and update this Community Management Plan

5. Always Improving

5.1 Listening to Our Members

We do not guess what our members need; we ask them. By combining surveys, direct conversations, and data, we get a full picture of how the community is feeling.

To capture this, we use:

  • Surveys: Quarterly checks on community spirit and an annual deep dive into member satisfaction
  • Direct feedback: Dedicated spaces on the platform, "office hours" with the Community Manager, and regular moderator calls
  • Open forums: Annual town halls and quarterly reviews where anyone can speak up
  • Data analysis: Weekly and monthly reviews of activity to spot trends that individual comments might miss

5.2 Experimenting with New Ideas

When we want to try something new — like a different event format or onboarding process — we treat it as an experiment, not a permanent change. We test ideas with small groups first, measure what happens, and only roll them out if they actually work.

Every experiment follows a simple cycle:

The experiment cycle

  • Hypothesis: What are we trying to improve?
  • Design: How will we test it (and have we asked the community)?
  • Pilot: Run a small trial
  • Review: Look at the results and decide whether to keep, change, or drop it

5.3 Learning Never Stops

A community is only as strong as the people running it. We invest in learning at every level:

  • Staff: We stay sharp by attending conferences, following industry leaders, and learning from other human rights groups
  • Moderators: We offer ongoing training, peer learning, and an annual development day to recognise their vital role
  • Members: We provide community calls, mentorship, and updated guides to help everyone become more effective activists

5.4 Staying Ahead of the Curve

The digital world changes quickly. We actively monitor new laws like the Online Safety Act and keep an eye on digital security threats to ensure our community stays safe and compliant.
