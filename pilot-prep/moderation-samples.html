Sample Posts to Test Moderation

A set of test posts designed for copying into Discourse to evaluate the AI moderation system — covering legitimate activism, edge cases, and clearly inappropriate content.

About this document

This document provides sample posts for use as test content within the Discourse platform. Each post has been designed to probe a specific dimension of the AI moderation system — from clearly acceptable community engagement through to posts that should always be flagged. The intention is to support structured evaluation and discussion about where the system draws its lines.

Posts are organised into three categories: those that should pass moderation without intervention, edge cases that warrant human judgment and discussion, and posts that are clearly inappropriate and should be flagged by the system.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Category 1: Legitimate Activism and Discussion

These posts represent acceptable community engagement, even when emotionally charged or containing strong language. They should generally not be flagged by moderation systems.

Guidelines for Moderators

The following guidance supports human moderators in reviewing flagged content and evaluating the AI system's decisions.

When to Approve Posts

  • Posts that name specific harms and discuss solutions
  • Criticism of institutions, governments, or systems — even when forcefully expressed
  • Discussion of difficult topics (Gaza, migration, detention, corporate accountability)
  • Disagreement about campaign tactics or strategy between community members
  • Sharing of factual information with reliable sources
  • Strong emotional language when directed at systems rather than individuals
  • Passionate advocacy using emphatic but non-dehumanising language

When to Flag for Review

  • Generalised attacks on groups of people by nationality, immigration status, religion, or ethnicity
  • Unsubstantiated conspiratorial claims without evidence or sourcing
  • Personal harassment of specific forum members using insulting language
  • Suggestions of illegal or violent action
  • Attempts to recruit others for extremist causes
  • Repeated commercial spam or promotional links
  • Deliberate misinformation presented as fact without qualification
  • Obfuscated profanity used as a workaround to bypass content filters
  • Spam indicators: excessive capitalisation, urgency markers, limited availability claims, multiple punctuation marks

Critical Distinctions

Situation Outcome
Harsh language criticising systems Generally acceptable
Harassment of individuals Should be removed
Discussing difficult geopolitical situations Generally acceptable
Promoting extremism or violence Should be flagged
Using strong emotions in advocacy Generally acceptable
Combining emotions with dehumanisation Rule violation
Sharing factual information from credible sources Generally acceptable
Presenting unverified misinformation as established fact Should be flagged
Disagreement about tactics Healthy — should be protected
Personal attacks on community members Should be removed
Advocacy with profanity May be acceptable depending on context
Profanity paired with dehumanising language Should be removed

Specific Considerations for AIUK

  • Activism inherently involves strong language and emotional content — this is normal and reflects the seriousness of human rights violations.
  • Educational discussion about human rights violations, detention, torture, and corporate accountability will include disturbing content — this is appropriate.
  • Debate about campaign strategy and tactics is healthy and should be protected to maintain community engagement.
  • The critical line is crossed when posts attack individuals or groups rather than critique systems, policies, or institutions.
  • Testing should specifically evaluate the system's ability to distinguish between legitimate passionate advocacy and genuine policy violations.
  • Commercial spam and misinformation pose particular challenges in activist spaces where urgency and sharing are cultural norms.
