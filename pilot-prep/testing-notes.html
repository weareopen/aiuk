Platform Testing
Notes

Notes from user testing sessions conducted in January 2026 — five participants from the AIUK community tested the new platform, rating usability and sharing detailed feedback on navigation, features, and community needs.

About these notes

In January 2026 we recruited five representatives of the AIUK community to participate in testing of the new platform. Details of how the testing was conducted, including the script, can be found in the Platform testing plan with users (Jan 2026).

Notes from the sessions with each test participant can be found further down in this document.

This document is held in Google Docs and is accessible to project team members.

Original on Google Drive (unavailable after May 2026)

How usable is the Amnesty Community?

The System Usability Scale (SUS) provides a quick measure of perceived usability through 10 statements rated 1 (strongly disagree) to 5 (strongly agree), alternating positive and negative wording.

Scores convert to a 0–100 value: subtract 1 from positive items (1, 3, 5, 7, 9), subtract response from 5 for negative items (2, 4, 6, 8, 10), sum the adjustments (0–40 maximum), then multiply by 2.5.

Average SUS score: 83

An average of 83 exceeds the SUS benchmark of 68, placing the platform above average. Scores from 68–80 count as above average; 80–90 reach excellent levels. The platform therefore demonstrates strong usability overall, with four out of five scores at or above 70.

Participant 1

Secretary of a local Amnesty group

Participant 1

Session Notes

Previous Experience with Online Communities

Participant 1 primarily uses Facebook groups, rather than website forums. Within the Facebook groups for their reading group, they post:

  • Meeting and event details
  • Sign-up information and booking details for events
  • Newsletters and links to activities
  • Interesting articles about countries or issues relevant to the group's focus

Technical Confidence

On a scale of one to five, Participant 1 rated their confidence in adapting to new systems and technologies as "three or four". They noted that while it is not their area of expertise, the experience of the pandemic made learning new things easier for everyone.

Goals for the Amnesty Community Platform

Participant 1 expressed a desire for the Amnesty community platform to facilitate better communication between groups campaigning on the same issues. For example, their group focuses on Hong Kong, and they know other groups around the country are doing the same, but current communication is not easily accessible or strategic. They highlighted a similar need for groups campaigning for the same prisoners of conscience.

They also want a place to store resources and information that groups can follow up on, as currently, groups mostly share information during regular meetings but lack a repository for it afterward. For instance, they had to manually email a petition for Afghan women to interested people, and suggested having an "Afghan section" on the platform where petitions could be easily uploaded and downloaded. Overall, they want the platform to be more intentional.

Usability Testing — Task Results

Task 1 — Account & Profile Setup
Participant 1 registered an account, choosing activist networks they were interested in: Feminists, Children's work, Anti-Death Penalty, and Anti-Racism Network. They noted that the "Queer" network used to be called the "Rainbow Network".

Task 2 — Onboarding & Training
Participant 1 found their profile page and changed their profile image. They copied and pasted text into the "About me" section and selected "everyday rights" and "freedom of expression" as interests.

Task 3 — Navigation & Discovery
Participant 1 explored the community, looking at categories like "Onboarding and Training" and networks like "Anti-Death Penalty". While in the "Anti-Death Penalty" chat area, they found it confusing that local group newsletters — such as a Bognor Regis newsletter — were posted there, as they expected to see the project's own monthly newsletters. They eventually moved on to the "Anti-Racism" network, noting they were automatically a member because they had ticked it during registration.

Task 4 — Content Consumption
Participant 1 navigated to the "Welcome and introductions" topic. They viewed the replies in the "Tell us a bit about yourself" topic and indicated interest in a post using an emoji response.

Task 5 — Community Interaction
Participant 1 went to the Anti-Racism Network and found the topic "Online actions you've taken recently". They replied to a specific message about organising an online event with limited resources, copying and pasting a provided response.

Task 6 — Content Creation
Participant 1 started a new topic in the "General discussion" within the "Socialising and sharing" category. No problems.

Task 7 — Trust & Safety (optional)
Participant 1 located a post they felt was inappropriate or concerning in the "Ongoing situation in Gaza" topic within "Cafe Random". They used the "Flag" option, chose the most suitable option, and added a short explanation.

Overall Impressions and Suggestions

  • Country Coordinators / Regional Areas: They could see the networks (like anti-racism) but not any of the country coordinators or regional areas (e.g. South Asia). They suggested that these coordinators, who typically publish their own newsletters, would want a place to host them that groups could access. Participant 1 believes the newsletters should be publicly accessible since anyone can currently sign up for the mailing list, but that the platform should link to the public resource. Within the community platform, they would like chat functionality for discussions on prisoners of conscience by country. They think country coordinators would want to moderate who can join their platform sections.
  • General Group Section: They asked whether there could be a section generally for groups, beyond the regions and networks, where secretaries could share different ways of doing things.

Participant 2

Co-Chair of an Amnesty Network

Participant 2

Session Notes

Previous Experience with Online Communities

Participant 2 co-chairs an Amnesty network. Their previous experience with online forums or communities includes using a Discord channel for the Amnesty "My Body My Rights" campaign — relatively new at the time — for general communications and updates. Beyond Amnesty, their experience with online platforms is minimal, largely limited to Facebook (but not Facebook groups). They had been using Discord for less than a year, around eight months. The Discord setup includes channels for general updates, event-specific details, and general chat.

Technical Confidence

On a scale of one to five, they rated their confidence in adapting to new systems and technologies as a three, noting they are not the most proficient but "not totally terrible". They mentioned that an Amnesty team member had set up a Notion page, and they anticipated needing an induction and training for it, having found other management system platforms to look complicated without proper guidance.

Goals for the Amnesty Community Platform

Participant 2 listed several things they would want to achieve on the Amnesty community platform:

  • Better communication with network members, including updates
  • Access to resources throughout the organisation
  • Addressing the "silos" within the organisation regarding getting and sharing information, presentations, and even onboarding
  • Standardising onboarding to avoid each team creating its own, which they view as a waste of resources
  • Providing resources and links in one place, including both general Amnesty information and role-specific committee onboarding details. They noted that information is often discovered by accident rather than intention, and a centralised resource would be "invaluable".

Usability Testing — Task Results

Task 1 — Account & Profile Setup
No issues.

Task 2 — Onboarding & Training
Participant 2 initially found the profile update process "a little bit hidden" because clicking their profile picture brought up notifications first, which was unexpected. They also scrolled past the "My Interests" section multiple times.

Task 3 — Navigation & Discovery
Participant 2 explored categories and found a discussion titled "fundraising for medical aid for Palestinians" interesting. They mentioned that they would expect to know more about the origin of content, questioning the "Community Manager Amnesty stuff" account, which felt "bot-like". Participant 2 also noticed confusion in a single post that seemed to mix past and future tenses, leading them to suggest a "page break" if it included an update.

Task 4 — Content Consumption
Participant 2 located a reply in the "Tell us a bit about yourself" topic and indicated they found it helpful by using the bookmark feature.

Task 5 — Community Interaction
Participant 2 joined the Anti-Racism Network and replied to a message about organising online events with limited resources. After replying, they noted that the system automatically set them to "following this topic," which they were not expecting but found "useful" as they would receive notifications.

Task 6 — Content Creation
Participant 2 created a new topic in the General Discussion category. No issues.

Task 7 — Trust & Safety (optional)
Participant 2 found an inappropriate post in the "Cafe Random" thread and reported it using the flag feature. No issues.

System Usability Scale (SUS)

When filling out the SUS form, Participant 2 initially marked "strongly disagreed" for the statement "I thought the platform was easy to use" when they meant "strongly agreed," highlighting the need to verbalise responses. They disagreed that the system was unnecessarily complex, and strongly disagreed that they would need technical support.

They mostly agreed that most people would learn to use it quickly, but expressed concern for older local group members (70–80 year olds) who may struggle with technology. It was noted that the platform, Discourse, allows for almost entire interaction via email — a feature chosen specifically to address this concern.

Overall Impressions and Suggestions

Participant 2 praised the platform's spacing and layout and the use of tags, which they felt would be useful for cross-cutting themes and discovering related content. They suggested a "tight remit" for tag usage, possibly allowing members to suggest tags for moderator approval to prevent spelling mistakes and duplicate work.

They liked the geographic aspect of the platform, which maps local groups. Participant 2 questioned the utility of the map function on their network's page, as their network does not have a geographic location. Finally, they wanted a way to narrow down the content on their network page, suggesting a way to organise or filter discussions by type, such as for "MP letter templates" or "quick resources". They also confirmed that the calendar function, which integrates with Google and Apple, would be excellent.

Participant 2 then discussed using the platform for group chat, and the facilitator showed how that could be achieved in a future phase.

Participant 3

Activist Representative (North East region)

Participant 3

Session Notes

Previous Experience with Online Communities

Participant 3 mentioned using a forum related to a football club they support, as well as a band forum. They also describe themselves as a "lurker" on Reddit, reading content without an account.

Technical Confidence

When asked about their confidence level with adapting to new systems and technologies on a scale of one to five, Participant 3 replied "four".

Goals for the Amnesty Community Platform

Participant 3 stated they would like to:

  • Discuss issues, concerns, and get ideas from other activists
  • Use it to facilitate some aspects of meetings, such as keeping a record of discussions
  • Use a video conferencing feature, which they note is a recurring difficulty for local groups wanting to have a hybrid meeting because they "need to get a Teams account or a Zoom account or something like that"

Usability Testing — Task Results

Task 1 — Account & Profile Setup
Participant 3 shared their thoughts while registering, noting that the username is the main way someone would mention them on the platform, and asking what the postcode (which is only available to admins) would be used for.

They also provided feedback on the dark mode, noting that some links in the "let's change the world together" box are the same colour as the background, making them difficult to read. They were suggested to switch to light mode, which they did.

Task 2 — Onboarding & Training
Participant 3 updated their profile, including changing their profile image and selecting "everyday rights" and "freedom of expression" as interests. No issues after some initial exploration.

Task 3 — Navigation & Discovery
Participant 3 navigated the platform, noting that it looks like a forum with different boards. They did not use the home page with the category navigation, but rather the menus and search functionality.

They found a discussion topic about the "ongoing situation in Gaza" by using the hamburger menu to select "topics".

Task 4 — Content Consumption
Participant 3 expressed some confusion about how "topics, posts, and categories relate to each other".

After selecting a reply, they were asked how they would indicate that they found it helpful or interesting. They selected a "raised fist" emoji but discussed the uncertainty of which emoji is idiomatic for the situation, and the common preference in forums they have used to reply with text rather than an emoji reaction.

Task 5 — Community Interaction
Participant 3 joined the "Anti-Racism Network," found the topic "online actions you've taken recently," and replied to a message. They noted that the reply uses a pop-up modal, which they describe as unique, and said that if it were a "more standard forum then typically not".

Task 6 — Content Creation
Participant 3 started a new topic in the "general discussion" subcategory of "socialising and sharing". They questioned whether their new post is visible to other people, stating that in a "well-designed" forum, there would be a notice if the post was being held for moderation.

The facilitator explained that the platform, Discourse, uses trust levels, which were partially skipped for Participant 3 via the invite.

Task 7 — Trust & Safety (optional)
Participant 3 was asked to find and report an inappropriate or concerning message. They found the report function and completed the task.

Overall Impressions and Suggestions

  • Terminology: They suggested changing platform-specific language (like "categories" and "topics") to more standard forum terms (like "boards," "subboards," and "threads") to avoid confusion, noting that the current terms have meant different things on other platforms.
  • Navigation: It was observed that Participant 3, unlike others tested, immediately used the menu and search rather than scrolling down the homepage, where all categories are listed. They agreed a splash page explaining the "ontology" (how categories and topics relate) would be helpful.
  • Tags: Participant 3 mentioned noticing the feature for tags and discussed different approaches to organising them, from a pre-created taxonomy to a "folksonomy approach".
  • Events / Calendar: Participant 3 noticed the events feature. They suggested a critical use case would be subscribing to events related to a particular campaign (e.g. anti-death penalty) within a specific region, and noted this might be possible at a future phase of the project with custom development using the first part of the postcode.
  • AI Moderator: The facilitator shared that the platform uses an AI moderator for the first pass on new posts, enforcing community guidelines and hiding inappropriate content until a human moderator reviews it. Participant 3 agreed with the decision not to make the AI prominent. They suggested being "selective" about when notifications are sent to users whose post is held for moderation, to avoid making users "feel worse" if they posted "in anger".
  • Pop-up Modal for Replies: Participant 3 noted that the pop-up modal for replies is "very different" to any other UX they have used, and they do not think it is a great use of "screen real estate". The facilitator explained that it allows for easier quoting and scrolling through long threads whilst composing a reply.
  • Email Integration: The facilitator explained that Discourse allows users to interact with threads almost entirely from their email inbox, which might be useful for the "55 plus demographic". Participant 3 asked how it handles email signatures and formatting.
  • Hybrid Meetings: Participant 3 reiterated the challenge that local groups face with the cost of meeting platforms like Zoom or Teams for hybrid meetings. The facilitator mentioned that Jitsi, an open-source video conferencing tool, integrates easily with Discourse but would require a Jitsi server — a potential phase two addition.
  • Screen Readers: Participant 3 asked about compatibility with screen readers for a member of their group. The facilitator confirmed it should be compatible with all main ones but noted that some issues with dropdowns are currently being addressed by Discourse.
  • Trust Levels: Participant 3 asked whether the trust level system is exposed to users. The facilitator explained that tool tips show what a user is allowed to do and what is required to reach the next level.

Participant 4

Co-Activism Representative & Local Group Membership Secretary

Participant 4

Session Notes

Previous Experience with Online Communities

Participant 4 uses Zoom frequently to organise and join meetings. They also use Facebook and WhatsApp regularly for organising. They are on Instagram but do not like it and have "essentially come off X since Elon Musk had anything to do with it".

Technical Confidence

When asked to rate their confidence with adapting to new systems and technologies on a scale of one to five, they initially said five to six, thinking it was out of ten, but then settled on two and a half to three out of five.

Goals for the Amnesty Community Platform

Participant 4 stated they would like to use the Amnesty community platform to:

  • Contact all the northern groups more easily
  • Provide a platform for the groups to discuss things together
  • Reach out to other activist networks because at the moment, groups tend to "very much work in silos" (e.g. local groups, university groups, death penalty network, feminist network) and "don't know what each other's doing"

Usability Testing — Task Results

Task 1 — Account & Profile Setup
The facilitator provided a link for Participant 4 to register and explained that the account would be deleted immediately after the testing. Participant 4 used their personal email address and set up their account without issue.

Task 2 — Onboarding & Training
Participant 4 was asked to find their profile page, which they struggled to find at first. After a nudge, they managed to change their profile image, and find the "About me" section to copy and paste text provided in the chat. They then selected "Everyday rights" and "freedom of expression" as interests and saved their changes.

Task 3 — Navigation & Discovery
Participant 4 explored the site and found the Anti-Death Penalty network. They also asked questions about various parts of the site.

Task 4 — Content Consumption
Participant 4 was asked to go to the "Socialising and sharing" category, then "Welcome and introductions," and show how they would indicate a reply was helpful or interesting. They chose an emoji reaction which did not cause them any problems.

Task 5 — Community Interaction
Participant 4 found this task straightforward. They replied to a message in the topic "online actions you've taken recently" with text provided in the chat.

Task 6 — Content Creation
Participant 4 had no problem starting a new topic in the "General discussion" area of "Socialising and sharing" using a provided title and body text.

Task 7 — Trust & Safety (optional)
Participant 4 successfully found an inappropriate or concerning message and reported it to the moderators. They chose the flag option and provided a description from the chat window.

Overall Impressions and Suggestions

Whilst filling out the System Usability Scale, Participant 4 noted that they would "probably use it quite frequently", but that it would take a bit of time to learn how to navigate properly, rating it a three out of five for complexity. They expressed concern about how easy elderly members of local groups would find the platform.

During the discussion following the tasks, Participant 4 inquired about event visibility and announcements:

  • Event Visibility: Events posted by a local group will automatically appear in their local category, their regional category, and across the entire site. The regional calendar is useful for seeing all events in the area.
  • Announcements: Only users with admin, site-wide moderation, or category moderation privileges can send announcements. A category moderator (e.g. for a North West group) can send an announcement to everyone in their area. To send an announcement to a different network (like the Feminist Network), a user would have to ask the network's moderator or a site-wide moderator.

Other site features shown were the Training calendar and the Map feature, which shows the locations of groups currently participating in the pilot.

The facilitator clarified that the platform is purely for the "Amnesty UK activist community" and requires a login. Information for the general public will be on the Amnesty website.

Participant 5

Activism Representative for Scotland

Participant 5

Session Notes

Previous Experience with Online Communities

Participant 5 has experience with online communities, mainly using Reddit and Discord, as well as other platforms like Trello. These platforms are typically used in their spare time because Amnesty does not have its own platform at present. Scotland's Amnesty International UK activists have a WhatsApp chat group.

Local groups have their own Facebook or Instagram pages, creating an online presence, but it is not structured like a forum. Communication for Amnesty often happens via email, WhatsApp, or Signal.

Technical Confidence

When asked about their confidence in adapting to new systems and technologies, on a scale of one to five (five being most confident), Participant 5 rated themselves a five.

Goals for the Amnesty Community Platform

Participant 5 sees a need for an Amnesty community platform to address several missing elements, including:

  • Public Debate: They believe there is a lack of public debate among members. Groups often receive direction solely from the central office. Members who believe the ongoing strategy is not working well lack a way to communicate this or discuss it with other groups.
  • Resource Sharing / Collaboration: Many local groups perform the same tasks but in different ways, leading to wasted effort and resources because they are not learning from each other's positive experiences. For example, during a human rights film festival, local groups communicated independently with the central office, asking the same questions and performing the same actions. If they could communicate directly, they could help each other and save work.
  • Technical Help: Some groups lack the technical skills needed for things like online or hybrid meetings. The Amnesty help desk sometimes works, but sometimes cannot provide this kind of assistance.
  • Structured Online Presence: Local groups' online presences (social media, websites) often rely on a single person, meaning activities stop if that person is busy or leaves. They suggest a more structured, centralised way of sharing, possibly using a regional page (like for Scotland) that local groups contribute to. This would ensure the page is regularly updated and helps build followers even if a local group stops.

Usability Testing — Task Results

Task 1 — Account & Profile Setup
When clicking the activation link, the page was blank and went to the bottom, which was unexpected. Despite this, Participant 5 found the site "very navigable" and easy to understand and move around. They felt the platform was more structured than they expected — seeing it as a middle ground between a proper forum with elaborate topics and a chat.

Task 2 — Onboarding & Training
Participant 5 successfully changed their profile picture, updated their bio, and expressed their interests. They thought that 'UK' should be at the top of the 'location' drop-down menu.

Task 3 — Navigation & Discovery
Participant 5 had no problem navigating the site, although they noted the platform had "a lot of white" and that the characters/font size were "a little bit small". They also suggested that the maximum width could be wider, as there seemed to be a lot of empty space.

Task 4 — Content Consumption
Participant 5 successfully bookmarked a contribution to the discussion, and found their bookmarks (after a small prompt) in the user menu.

Task 5 — Community Interaction
This task caused Participant 5 no issues.

Task 6 — Content Creation
Likewise, this was very straightforward for Participant 5.

Task 7 — Trust & Safety (optional)
Participant 5 successfully reported a post by flagging it as illegal.

Overall Impressions and Suggestions

Participant 5 noted that the bookmarks and other profile options were a bit hidden and not immediately clear after clicking the profile icon. They expressed a personal preference for a persistent left-hand menu, similar to the admin dashboard. This would allow them to quickly access their interests and networks.

They suggested that the general discussion part should be more structured, with a few broad general topics (like campaigns, countries, strategy) and then subtopics for focused discussion. They prefer this over using tags, which they find confusing.

Key themes across testing sessions

Across all five sessions, several consistent themes emerged that will inform the pilot and subsequent development.

🔗

Cross-group connection

Every participant cited wanting to connect with groups and networks beyond their own — currently happening in silos via email, WhatsApp, or not at all.

📋

Resource repositories

A recurring need: a structured, searchable place to store and retrieve resources, petitions, newsletters, and event information.

♿

Accessibility and inclusion

Multiple participants raised concerns about older or less confident members. Email integration, clear navigation, and screen-reader support are critical.

Overall usability verdict

With an average SUS score of 83 — well above the industry benchmark of 68 — the platform performed strongly. Four of five participants scored it 70 or above, indicating solid perceived usability even at this early testing stage. The key areas for refinement are onboarding clarity, profile discoverability, and navigation terminology.

Issues to address before pilot

  • Profile page is difficult to find for less experienced users — consider a more prominent "Edit your profile" prompt post-login
  • Dark mode has contrast issues with link colours
  • Page scrolls to the bottom on login — unexpected and disorienting
  • Local group content appearing in network topic feeds needs configuration attention
  • The welcome banner dismiss button is too small
  • Platform terminology ("categories," "topics") is unfamiliar — consider onboarding tooltips or a glossary
  • The pop-up modal for replies is non-standard — clear signposting or onboarding guidance needed
  • UK should be at the top of the location drop-down
